Unit 1: What is Life?

Unit Overview: In this unit, we embark on the most fundamental question in biology: What does it mean to be alive? You might think the answer is simple—a dog is alive, a rock is not. But what about a virus? What about a seed that has lain dormant for a thousand years? As we explore the history of biological discovery, from ancient philosophical musings to modern molecular biology, we will see that the line between the living and non-living is often blurrier than we expect.

Saskatchewan Context

As we study life, we acknowledge that we are learning on Treaty lands—the traditional territories of the Nêhiyawak, Anihšināpēk, Dakota, Lakota, and Nakoda, and the homeland of the Métis/Michif Nation. Indigenous ways of knowing teach us that "life" is not just a biological checklist but a web of relationships (wâhkôhtowin) connecting all things, animate and inanimate.

Chapter 1: The Changing Scientific Understandings of Life

1.1 Personal Definitions of Life

Learning Objective: Identify your personal definition of life and its characteristics, recognizing that these definitions can be fluid and context-dependent.

If you were asked to define "life" right now, what criteria would you list? Most biology students recall the acronym MRS GREN (Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition). This mnemonic serves as a useful starting point, but as we advance, we find exceptions. A sterile mule cannot reproduce, but is alive. Fire grows and consumes energy, but is not alive. Biologists generally agree on a set of seven characteristics that, when taken together, define life:

  1. Order: Life is characterized by highly organized structures (molecules to tissues).
  2. Reproduction: The ability to produce offspring.
  3. Growth and Development: Organisms grow according to genetic instructions.
  4. Energy Processing: Metabolism—transforming energy to perform work.
  5. Response to the Environment: Responsiveness to environmental stimuli.
  6. Regulation (Homeostasis): Maintaining a stable internal environment (e.g., body temp).
  7. Evolutionary Adaptation: Populations evolve over generations to suit environments.

Formative Check

  • 1. Why is "movement" alone not sufficient to define life? Give an example of a non-living thing that moves.
  • 2. Which characteristic does a sterile mule fail to meet? Does this mean it is not alive?

1.2 Diverse Perspectives on Life

Learning Objective: Distinguish among scientific, First Nations, Métis, Inuit, and other cultural perspectives with respect to the question of what constitutes life.
Western Scientific Perspective:

Western science traditionally tends to be reductionist. This approach seeks to understand complex systems by breaking them down into smaller parts (atoms, molecules, cells). Life is often seen as a mechanistic process driven by chemistry and physics.

Indigenous Perspectives:

Indigenous knowledge systems are often holistic. This worldview emphasizes the relationships between all things. In Cree (nêhiyawêwin), objects are often classified by animate vs. inanimate grammar rather than a biological checklist. A rock (asiniy) is grammatically animate, acknowledging its spirit and role in the ecosystem.

1.3 The Evolution of the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Explore how scientific definitions of life have changed over time, specifically the shift from spontaneous generation to biogenesis.

For centuries, the belief was Spontaneous Generation (Abiogenesis)—the idea that life could arise from non-living matter (e.g., maggots from meat). Aristotle and Van Helmont (who had a "recipe" for mice) were early proponents. It took centuries of inquiry to shift to Biogenesis—the principle that living things can only come from other living things.

1.4 The Gray Area: Viruses, Viroids, and Prions

Learning Objective: Debate whether viruses, viroids, and prions are alive based on biological criteria.
1. Viruses

DNA/RNA in a protein coat. They hijack hosts to replicate. Alive or obligate parasites?

2. Viroids

Short circular RNA without coats. They infect plants and challenge definitions of genetic "life".

3. Prions

Misfolded proteins with no genetic material. They replicate by conversion, causing diseases like CWD.

Saskatchewan Context: Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD)

Saskatchewan is at the forefront of prion research due to Chronic Wasting Disease in deer and elk populations. Prions persist in soil for years, challenging our idea of "infectious" life.

1.5 Giants of Biology

Learning Objective: Recognize the contributions of scientists like Redi, Pasteur, Miller, Urey, and Margulis.
  • Francesco Redi (1668): Disproved spontaneous generation of maggots using gauze-covered jars.
  • Louis Pasteur (1859): Disproved microscopic spontaneous generation using Swan-Neck Flasks.
  • Miller & Urey (1953): Simulated early Earth to synthesize amino acids from inorganic chemicals.
  • Lynn Margulis (1967): Proposed Endosymbiosis Theory—cooperation as a driver of evolution.

1.6 Technology and the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Examine the impact of cell theory and advances in microscopy on scientific definitions of life.

Microscopy revealed the Cell Theory: the cell is the basic unit of life. Electron microscopes allowed us to see viruses and organelles, blurring the line again. In Saskatchewan, the Canadian Light Source (CLS) synchrotron in Saskatoon uses brilliant light to peer into the molecular structure of life.
