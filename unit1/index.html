Unit 1: What is Life?

Unit Overview: In this unit, we embark on the most fundamental question in biology: What does it mean to be alive? You might think the answer is simple—a dog is alive, a rock is not. But what about a virus? What about a seed that has lain dormant for a thousand years? As we explore the history of biological discovery, from ancient philosophical musings to modern molecular biology, we will see that the line between the living and non-living is often blurrier than we expect.

Saskatchewan Context

As we study life, we acknowledge that we are learning on Treaty lands—the traditional territories of the Nêhiyawak, Anihšināpēk, Dakota, Lakota, and Nakoda, and the homeland of the Métis/Michif Nation. Indigenous ways of knowing teach us that "life" is not just a biological checklist but a web of relationships (wâhkôhtowin) connecting all things, animate and inanimate. We will explore these perspectives alongside Western scientific definitions.

1.1 Personal Definitions of Life

Learning Objective: Identify your personal definition of life and its characteristics, recognizing that these definitions can be fluid and context-dependent.

If you were asked to define "life" right now, what criteria would you list? Most biology students recall the acronym MRS GREN from earlier grades (Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition). This mnemonic serves as a useful starting point, but as we advance in biology, we find that these simple checklists often encounter exceptions in the natural world. For instance, consider the sterile mule: it is undeniably alive, yet it cannot reproduce. Fire consumes energy, grows, and spreads, mimicking reproduction, yet we know it is not alive. Crystals can grow and organize into predictable structures, but they lack metabolism. These exceptions force us to seek a more robust definition.

Biologists generally agree on a set of seven characteristics that, when taken together, define life. To be considered "alive" in a scientific sense, an entity usually must exhibit all of the following:

  1. Order: Life is characterized by highly organized structures. This isn't just about symmetry; it's about complexity at every level. In a mammal, for example, atoms form molecules, which organize into organelles, which build cells. These cells form specialized tissues (like muscle or nerve tissue), which coordinate to form organs (like the heart), and finally organ systems. This hierarchy of structure allows for complex functions that non-living matter cannot achieve.
  2. Reproduction: The ability to produce offspring is fundamental to the continuation of life. Whether it is a single-celled bacteria dividing in two or a complex mammal giving birth, life possesses the machinery to duplicate itself.
  3. Growth and Development: Organisms grow and develop according to specific instructions coded in their genes. Your DNA contains the blueprint that directed your growth from a single cell into a complex multicellular organism, ensuring you developed human characteristics rather than those of a frog or a fern.
  4. Energy Processing: All organisms take in energy and transform it to perform all of life's activities. A plant captures solar energy to build sugars, while a bison eats that grass to power its muscles. This flow of energy, known as metabolism, is the engine of life.
  5. Response to the Environment: Living things respond to environmental stimuli. A venus flytrap snaps shut when an insect touches its sensory hairs; you squint when you walk into bright sunlight. This responsiveness is critical for survival.
  6. Regulation (Homeostasis): Even when the external environment changes drastically, organisms have mechanisms to maintain a stable internal environment. Consider a polar bear in the Arctic: despite freezing temperatures outside, its body maintains a constant internal temperature through metabolic heat production and insulation. This regulatory balance is called homeostasis.
  7. Evolutionary Adaptation: Over generations, populations evolve. Individuals with traits best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing those advantageous traits to offspring. The camouflage of a snowshoe hare, which turns white in winter, is a result of this evolutionary process.

However, your personal definition might differ. Is life purely biological, or is there a consciousness component? As we move through this course, keep your initial definition in mind and see if it evolves.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Western Red Lily vs. The River

Consider the Western Red Lily (Lilium philadelphicum), Saskatchewan's floral emblem. It clearly fits the biological criteria: it grows, photosynthesizes (energy processing), and reproduces via seeds. Now consider the South Saskatchewan River. It moves, it changes the landscape, and it supports life. In a strict biological sense, it is non-living. However, in many Indigenous worldviews, the river is considered an animate spirit or force with its own agency.

1.2 Diverse Perspectives on Life

Learning Objective: Distinguish among scientific, First Nations, Métis, Inuit, and other cultural perspectives with respect to the question of what constitutes life.

Science is one powerful way of knowing, but it is not the only way. Understanding life requires us to look through multiple lenses.

Western Scientific Perspective:

Western science traditionally tends to be reductionist. This approach seeks to understand complex systems by breaking them down into their smaller, fundamental parts. To understand a heart, a reductionist might study the muscle tissue, then the individual cells, then the proteins within those cells, and finally the atoms that make up the proteins. In this view, life is often seen as a mechanistic process driven by the laws of chemistry and physics—a complex machine that, if we understand all the gears, we can understand the whole.

Indigenous Perspectives (First Nations and Métis):

In contrast, Indigenous knowledge systems are often holistic. This worldview emphasizes the web of relationships between all things. Rather than isolating parts, it looks at the whole system and how everything connects. In many First Nations languages, the grammatical structure itself reflects this view. Objects are not just classified as "living vs. non-living" in the biological sense, but often by animate vs. inanimate grammar.

  • For example, in Cree (nêhiyawêwin), a rock (asiniy) is grammatically animate. This classification acknowledges that the rock has a spirit, a history, and an active role in the ecosystem, even if it doesn't breathe or metabolize in a Western scientific sense.
  • Wâhkôhtowin (Kinship): This central Cree concept describes the interconnectedness of all beings. Life is not defined by isolation but by connection. You are considered alive because you are in relationship with the air you breathe, the land you stand on, and the water you drink. Severing these connections diminishes life.

Saskatchewan Connection: Elders' Wisdom

In Saskatchewan, Elders often speak of the land as a living relation. When harvesting medicinal plants like sage or sweetgrass, it is traditional protocol to offer tobacco. This act is not just symbolic; it is an acknowledgement that the plant is a living being that is giving its life to help you. It establishes a reciprocal relationship—a contract of mutual respect—that is absent in a purely mechanical view of biology.

1.3 The Evolution of the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Explore how scientific definitions of life have changed over time, specifically the shift from spontaneous generation to biogenesis.

Our scientific understanding of life has not always been stable. For centuries, the prevailing belief was Spontaneous Generation (or Abiogenesis)—the idea that life could arise spontaneously from non-living matter. This seemed logical based on casual observation: if you left meat out, maggots appeared; if you left grain in a dark corner, mice eventually "generated."

  • Aristotle (384–322 BC): One of history's greatest thinkers, yet he proposed that aphids arise from the dew on plants because he never saw them mate.
  • Jean-Baptiste van Helmont (1600s): He famously published a "recipe" for creating mice: place a dirty shirt and some wheat in a dark container, and in 21 days, he claimed, mice would appear. We now know the mice were simply attracted to the food, but at the time, it was accepted science.

It took centuries of inquiry and the refinement of the Scientific Method to shift the paradigm to Biogenesis—the principle that living things can only come from other living things (reproduction). This shift required rigorous experimental design, using controls and manipulating variables, to disprove the "common sense" observations of the time.

Saskatchewan Connection

While early European scientists were debating spontaneous generation, Indigenous peoples on the Great Plains maintained deep, observational knowledge of life cycles. Their survival depended on it. They knew that bison populations were driven by breeding seasons and migration patterns, not spontaneous appearance from the earth. This practical ecological knowledge is a form of rigorous scientific observation that predates many European discoveries.

1.4 The Gray Area: Viruses, Viroids, and Prions

Learning Objective: Debate whether viruses, viroids, and prions are alive based on biological criteria.

Nature rarely fits into perfect boxes. As we refine our definitions of life, we encounter entities that exist on the blurry edge, challenging our criteria.

1. Viruses

These are genetic code (DNA or RNA) wrapped in a protein coat. They have no metabolism and cannot reproduce on their own. They must hijack a host cell's machinery to copy themselves. Are they alive? They evolve and adapt, but they fail the criteria for independent reproduction and metabolism. They are often termed obligate intracellular parasites.

2. Viroids

Simpler than viruses, these are short, circular strands of RNA without even a protein coat. They primarily infect plants, like the potato spindle tuber viroid, causing disease by interfering with gene regulation. They strip "life" down to bare genetic information.

3. Prions

These are the most puzzling. Prions are misfolded proteins—they contain no DNA or RNA at all. Yet, they cause disease by inducing normal proteins in the brain to misfold. They "replicate" by conversion, leading to fatal neurodegenerative diseases. They challenge the very idea that life requires genetic material.

Saskatchewan Connection: Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD)

Saskatchewan is unfortunately at the forefront of global prion research due to the prevalence of Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD) in our wild cervid populations (mule deer, white-tailed deer, and elk). CWD is a fatal nervous system disease caused by a prion. Unlike a virus that dies outside a host, prions can persist in the soil for years. When a deer grazes on contaminated land, the prion enters its system, eventually causing the "wasting" symptoms. This real-world crisis illustrates how a "non-living" protein can behave like an infectious agent, devastating living populations.

1.5 Giants of Biology

Learning Objective: Recognize the contributions of scientists like Redi, Pasteur, Miller, Urey, and Margulis.

Science is rarely a solo endeavour; it is a cumulative human effort where each discovery builds upon the last.

  • Francesco Redi (1668): He struck the first major blow to spontaneous generation. By simply covering jars of meat with gauze, he proved that maggots came from fly eggs, not the meat itself. It was an elegant example of using a controlled variable.
  • Louis Pasteur (1859): Two centuries later, the debate still raged over microscopic life. Pasteur designed the famous Swan-Neck Flask. This genius design allowed vital air to enter the flask but trapped dust and microbes in the curved neck. The broth remained sterile for months, proving that "Omne vivum ex vivo" (All life comes from life).
  • Miller & Urey (1953): Moving to the 20th century, these scientists asked: "If life comes from life, where did the first life come from?" They simulated Earth's primordial atmosphere (ammonia, methane, hydrogen) and added sparks (lightning). Within a week, complex organic amino acids formed from simple inorganic chemicals, suggesting how the building blocks of life might have begun.
  • Lynn Margulis (1967): She revolutionized our view of cell evolution with the Endosymbiosis Theory. She proposed that complex cells (eukaryotes) evolved when one simple cell engulfed another but didn't digest it. Instead, they formed a partnership. The engulfed bacteria became mitochondria (the powerhouse) and chloroplasts. It was a radical idea that cooperation, not just competition, drives evolution.

1.6 Technology and the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Examine the impact of the cell theory and advances in microscopy on scientific definitions of life.

Our definition of life is fundamentally limited by what we can perceive. Before the 1600s, "life" was anything big enough to see and move. The invention of the light microscope changed everything. Suddenly, a drop of pond water was revealed to be a bustling metropolis of tiny organisms. This led to the Cell Theory: the realization that the cell is the basic unit of structure and function, and that all life is composed of cells.

Later, the invention of the Electron Microscope in the 1930s allowed us to see even deeper, revealing viruses and the complex internal organelles of cells. Each technological leap blurs the line again—allowing us to see the mechanical machinery of life (ribosomes, DNA strands) operating at a molecular level.

Saskatchewan Connection: Canadian Light Source (CLS)

Located in Saskatoon, the Canadian Light Source (CLS) represents the cutting edge of this technological evolution. It is a synchrotron that accelerates electrons to near light speed to produce incredibly brilliant beams of light. Saskatchewan researchers use this light to peer into the microscopic world with detail impossible with standard microscopes, studying everything from the molecular structure of wheat proteins to the internal damage caused by diseases.
