Unit 1: What is Life?

Unit Overview: In this unit, we embark on the most fundamental question in biology: What does it mean to be alive? You might think the answer is simple—a dog is alive, a rock is not. But what about a virus? What about a seed that has lain dormant for a thousand years? As we explore the history of biological discovery, from ancient philosophical musings to modern molecular biology, we will see that the line between the living and non-living is often blurrier than we expect.

Saskatchewan Context

As we study life, we acknowledge that we are learning on Treaty lands—the traditional territories of the Nêhiyawak, Anihšināpēk, Dakota, Lakota, and Nakoda, and the homeland of the Métis/Michif Nation. Indigenous ways of knowing teach us that "life" is not just a biological checklist but a web of relationships (wâhkôhtowin) connecting all things, animate and inanimate. We will explore these perspectives alongside Western scientific definitions.

Chapter 1: The Changing Scientific Understandings of Life

1.1 Personal Definitions of Life

Learning Objective: Identify your personal definition of life and its characteristics, recognizing that these definitions can be fluid and context-dependent.

If you were asked to define "life" right now, what criteria would you list? Most biology students recall the acronym MRS GREN from earlier grades (Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition). However, as we advance in biology, we find that these simple checklists often encounter exceptions.

  • Mules cannot reproduce, yet they are alive.
  • Fire consumes energy, grows, and reproduces (spreads), yet it is not alive.
  • Crystals can grow and organize, but they lack metabolism.

Biologists generally agree on a more robust set of characteristics, though debates continue. To be considered "alive" in a scientific sense, an entity usually must exhibit all of the following:

  1. Order: Highly organized structures (cells).
  2. Reproduction: The ability to produce offspring.
  3. Growth and Development: Controlled by inherited information (DNA).
  4. Energy Processing: Taking in energy to power activities (metabolism).
  5. Response to the Environment: Reacting to stimuli.
  6. Regulation (Homeostasis): Maintaining stable internal conditions.
  7. Evolutionary Adaptation: Populations change over time to survive in their environment.

However, your personal definition might differ. Is life purely biological, or is there a consciousness component? As we move through this course, keep your initial definition in mind and see if it evolves.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Western Red Lily vs. The River

Consider the Western Red Lily (Lilium philadelphicum), Saskatchewan's floral emblem. It clearly fits the biological criteria: it grows, photosynthesizes (energy processing), and reproduces via seeds. Now consider the South Saskatchewan River. It moves, it changes the landscape, and it supports life. In a strict biological sense, it is non-living. However, in many Indigenous worldviews, the river is considered an animate spirit or force with its own agency.

  • 1. Why is "movement" alone not a sufficient characteristic to define life? Give an example of a non-living thing that moves.
  • 2. Which of the seven characteristics of life does a sterile mule fail to meet? Does this mean it is not alive? Explain.

1.2 Diverse Perspectives on Life

Learning Objective: Distinguish among scientific, First Nations, Métis, Inuit, and other cultural perspectives with respect to the question of what constitutes life.

Science is one powerful way of knowing, but it is not the only way.

Western Scientific Perspective:

Western science tends to be reductionist. It seeks to understand life by breaking it down into smaller parts—organs, tissues, cells, molecules, and atoms. In this view, life is a mechanistic process driven by chemistry and physics.

Indigenous Perspectives (First Nations and Métis):

Indigenous knowledge is often holistic. It views the world as a web of relationships. In many First Nations languages, objects are classified not by "living vs. non-living" in the biological sense, but by animate vs. inanimate grammar.

  • For example, in Cree (nêhiyawêwin), a rock (asiniy) is grammatically animate. This acknowledges that the rock has a spirit, a history, and a role in the ecosystem.
  • Wâhkôhtowin (Kinship): This Cree concept describes the interconnectedness of all beings. Life is not defined by isolation but by connection.

Saskatchewan Connection: Elders' Wisdom

In Saskatchewan, Elders often speak of the land as a living relation. When harvesting medicinal plants like sage or sweetgrass, it is traditional protocol to offer tobacco. This is an acknowledgement that the plant is a living being that is giving its life to help you, establishing a reciprocal relationship that is absent in a purely mechanical view of biology.

1.3 The Evolution of the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Explore how scientific definitions of life have changed over time, specifically the shift from spontaneous generation to biogenesis.

Our understanding of life has not always been stable. For centuries, humans believed in Spontaneous Generation (Abiogenesis)—the idea that life could arise from non-living matter.

  • Aristotle (384–322 BC): Proposed that aphids arise from dew on plants.
  • Jean-Baptiste van Helmont (1600s): Published a "recipe" for mice involving a dirty shirt and wheat.

It took centuries of inquiry and the development of the Scientific Method to shift the paradigm to Biogenesis—the principle that living things can only come from other living things.

Saskatchewan Connection

While early European scientists were debating spontaneous generation, Indigenous peoples on the Great Plains had deep, observational knowledge of life cycles. They knew that bison populations were driven by breeding seasons and migration, not spontaneous appearance.

Formative Check

  • 1. What was "Spontaneous Generation"? Give one historical example of this belief.
  • 2. Define "Biogenesis."

1.4 The Gray Area: Viruses, Viroids, and Prions

Learning Objective: Debate whether viruses, viroids, and prions are alive based on biological criteria.

Nature rarely fits into perfect boxes. There are entities that exist on the edge of life, challenging our definitions.

1. Viruses

Genetic code wrapped in protein. Cannot reproduce on their own. Obligate intracellular parasites.

2. Viroids

Short, circular strands of RNA without a protein coat. Primarily infect plants.

3. Prions

Misfolded proteins. No DNA or RNA. Induce other proteins to misfold (e.g., CWD).

Saskatchewan Connection: Chronic Wasting Disease (CWD)

Saskatchewan is at the forefront of prion research due to the prevalence of Chronic Wasting Disease in our wild mule deer, white-tailed deer, and elk populations. CWD is caused by a prion. It is fatal and spreads through direct contact or environmental contamination.

Formative Check

  • 1. Why are viruses considered "obligate intracellular parasites"?
  • 2. How does a prion differ from a virus in terms of structure?

1.5 Giants of Biology

Learning Objective: Recognize the contributions of scientists like Redi, Pasteur, Miller, Urey, and Margulis.

Science is a cumulative human endeavour.

  • Francesco Redi (1668): First major blow to spontaneous generation (maggots on meat experiment).
  • Louis Pasteur (1859): Used the Swan-Neck Flask to definitively prove "Omne vivum ex vivo" (All life comes from life).
  • Miller & Urey (1953): Simulated Earth's early atmosphere to show that organic amino acids could form from inorganic chemicals.
  • Lynn Margulis (1967): Proposed Endosymbiosis theory (mitochondria/chloroplasts were once free-living bacteria).

1.6 Technology and the Definition of Life

Learning Objective: Examine the impact of the cell theory and advances in microscopy on scientific definitions of life.

Our definition of life is limited by what we can see. The invention of the light microscope (1600s) led to the Cell Theory. Later, the electron microscope (1930s) allowed us to see viruses, blurring the line again.

Saskatchewan Connection: Canadian Light Source (CLS)

Located in Saskatoon, the CLS is a synchrotron that uses brilliant beams of light to view the microscopic world. Saskatchewan researchers use it to look at the molecular structure of proteins and plants, pushing the boundaries of how we understand biological structures.
