Chapter 4: Comparative Anatomy and Physiology

Vocabulary Builder

Every living organism faces the same fundamental challenges: acquiring energy, exchanging gases, defending against threats, and reproducing to ensure the survival of its lineage. Yet, when we look at the natural world, the solutions to these problems are staggeringly diverse. A pine tree and a timber wolf must both transport water and nutrients, but their internal machinery evolved on entirely different paths.

In this chapter, we transition from examining single, isolated cells to investigating how massive societies of cells cooperate. Anatomy is the study of an organism's physical structure—the "hardware." Physiology is the study of how those structures function—the "software." By comparing the anatomies and physiologies across different kingdoms, we can map the brilliant evolutionary adaptations that allow life to thrive in every corner of the Earth, including the harsh extremes of the Saskatchewan prairie.

4.1 Maintaining Homeostasis [BI30-OL2f, BI30-OL2g, BI30-OL2h]

Learning Objective: Identify how multicellular organisms maintain homeostasis through processes such as biofeedback, fluid regulation, and thermoregulation, and respond to environmental stimuli.

Before investigating the specific anatomies of different organisms, we must understand the universal physiological goal they are all trying to achieve. Regardless of the chaos in the external environment, the cells inside an organism require strict, stable conditions to survive. This state of internal balance is called homeostasis. Organisms maintain homeostasis primarily through negative feedback loops, where a change in a physiological variable triggers a response that reverses the initial change (like a thermostat controlling a furnace).

1. Thermoregulation (Temperature Control)

Cellular enzymes only function within specific temperature ranges.

  • Ectotherms (Cold-blooded): Rely on environmental heat. A garter snake must bask on a warm rock to raise its internal temperature enough to digest its food.
  • Endotherms (Warm-blooded): Generate heat internally via metabolism. When a human gets cold, the brain triggers shivering (muscle contractions) to generate heat, and blood vessels in the skin constrict to keep warm blood near vital organs.

2. Fluid Regulation (Osmoregulation)

Organisms must balance water intake and loss. Mammals utilize complex kidneys to filter blood, excreting toxic urea in urine while reabsorbing vital water. Plants maintain fluid balance through transpiration—if the soil is dry, the plant will close its stomata to prevent water from escaping as vapor, though this halts photosynthesis.

3. Responding to Stimuli

Homeostasis requires detecting external changes. Plants exhibit tropisms—growing towards light (phototropism) or responding to gravity (gravitropism). Animals utilize complex nervous systems to detect light, sound, touch, and chemical pheromones, translating these external signals into internal hormonal responses.

Saskatchewan Connection: Freezing Solid

Maintaining homeostasis during a Saskatchewan winter is a profound physiological challenge. While endotherms like the Arctic Fox grow thick winter coats and increase their metabolic rate to stay warm, the Wood Frog utilizes a radical strategy. As temperatures drop below zero, the frog stops breathing and its heart stops. It allows up to 65% of the water in its body to freeze solid. To prevent the sharp ice crystals from shredding its cells, the frog floods its tissues with glucose (sugar) which acts as a natural biological antifreeze (cryoprotectant). In the spring, it thaws out and hops away!

4.2 Structure and Function Across Kingdoms [BI30-OL2a]

Learning Objective: Compare the anatomical structures and functions of organisms representative of several kingdoms, using dissections and/or simulations.

Multicellularity allows organisms to grow large, but size introduces a critical problem: not every cell is in contact with the outside environment. An organism must evolve specialized tissues and organs to transport materials, digest food, and provide structural support. Let us compare how different kingdoms tackle these physiological needs.

  • Protists: Though mostly unicellular, some like kelp (brown algae) are large and multicellular. They lack true roots, stems, or leaves. Because they live in water, they rely heavily on direct diffusion across their cell membranes for gas exchange and waste removal.

  • Fungi: Fungi do not have a digestive tract. They are composed of thread-like structures called hyphae, which form a mass called a mycelium. They exhibit extracellular digestion, secreting enzymes into their environment and absorbing the broken-down nutrients directly.

  • Plantae: Plants are anchored autotrophs. To acquire both sunlight (above ground) and water (below ground), they evolved a dual system. Vascular tissue is the plant's plumbing: xylem transports water up, and phloem transports sugars down. Gas exchange occurs through microscopic pores in the leaves called stomata.

  • Animalia: Animals are mobile heterotrophs, requiring massive amounts of energy. This necessitates highly specialized organ systems: complex digestive tracts to break down food internally, respiratory systems (lungs, gills) to acquire oxygen for cellular respiration, and a circulatory system (heart, blood vessels) to deliver materials to every cell.

Homologous vs. Analogous Structures

When comparing anatomy across different organisms, biologists often look for similarities to understand relationships. We will explore this concept in much greater depth in the Evolution unit, but a foundational introduction is useful here:

  • Homologous Structures: These are structures that share a similar underlying framework or origin, even if they have adapted to serve entirely different functions. This structural similarity suggests a shared common ancestor. Example: The arm of a human, the front leg of a horse, the flipper of a whale, and the wing of a bat all share the exact same basic bone structure (humerus, radius, ulna, carpals), despite being used for completely different tasks like grasping, running, swimming, and flying.
  • Analogous Structures: These are structures that perform a similar function but have entirely different underlying anatomies and evolutionary origins. They evolved independently to solve the same environmental challenge. Example: The wing of a bird (supported by bones and feathers) and the wing of an insect (supported by chitinous veins and membranes) both achieve flight, but they are not structurally related.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Prairie Wetland

A typical Saskatchewan prairie pothole (wetland) perfectly illustrates this cross-kingdom comparison. A Mallard duck (Animal) uses a complex respiratory system to dive for food. It feeds on Cattails (Plant), which use hollow structural tubes to transport oxygen down to their submerged roots. Breaking down the dead plant matter at the bottom of the slough are water molds and fungi, utilizing extracellular digestion to recycle nutrients back into the ecosystem.

4.3 Model Organisms in Research [BI30-OL2b]

Learning Objective: Critique the use of model organisms in scientific research, including ethical implications.

We cannot test new medical treatments or explore basic genetics directly on humans due to ethical and practical constraints. Instead, biologists use model organisms—non-human species that are extensively studied to understand particular biological phenomena, with the expectation that discoveries will provide insight into the workings of other organisms, including humans.

Why Choose a Specific Model?

Scientists select model organisms based on specific traits: short generation times, easy breeding in a lab, small size, and genetic similarities to humans. Common models include:

  • Escherichia coli (E. coli): A bacterium used for basic DNA and protein research.
  • Arabidopsis thaliana (Thale cress): A small weed with a fully mapped genome, serving as the universal model for plant biology and agriculture.
  • Drosophila melanogaster (Fruit fly): Ideal for genetics and inheritance studies because they breed quickly and share 60% of their DNA with humans.
  • Mus musculus (House mouse): A mammalian model crucial for studying human diseases, immunity, and behavior, as they share 85% of protein-coding genes with us.

The Ethical Imperative

The use of animal models carries a heavy ethical weight. The scientific community is guided by the 3Rs Principle to ensure humane treatment:

  1. Replacement: Can we use a non-animal alternative, like a computer simulation or cell culture?
  2. Reduction: Can we use fewer animals while still obtaining statistically valid results?
  3. Refinement: Can we modify procedures to minimize pain and distress?

4.4 Indigenous Perspectives on Research [BI30-OL2c]

Learning Objective: Research First Nations and Métis perspectives regarding the use of living things for scientific research.

Western science often views model organisms in a utilitarian way—as tools or resources to gather data. However, Indigenous knowledge systems approach the use of living things from a fundamentally different ethical standpoint, rooted in relationality.

Revisiting the Cree concept of Wâhkôhtowin (kinship), we recognize that animals, plants, and even the land are not objects, but relations. In many First Nations and Métis traditions, human beings are not considered superior to the animal world; rather, animals are viewed as teachers and equals. Therefore, using an animal for research is not an automatic human right—it is a taking of a life that demands deep respect, justification, and spiritual protocol.

Key Differences in Ethical Frameworks:
  • Protocol and Permission: Indigenous research often requires offering tobacco or participating in ceremony before taking from the land or utilizing an animal, acknowledging the sacrifice being made.
  • Holism vs. Isolation: Removing an animal from its ecosystem to a sterile lab environment breaks its relational web. Indigenous methodologies often prefer observational study within the natural environment.
  • Reciprocity: If an animal's life is used to gain knowledge, that knowledge must be used to heal or protect the community and the land, maintaining a balance.

Saskatchewan Connection: Evolving Research Ethics

Institutions like the University of Saskatchewan and the First Nations University of Canada are leading the way in integrating these perspectives. Modern research ethics boards are increasingly requiring scientists to consult with Indigenous Elders and Knowledge Keepers when their biological research impacts local wildlife, ensuring that scientific inquiry respects traditional lands and the dignity of the animal nations.

4.5 Reproductive Strategies [BI30-OL2d]

Learning Objective: Analyze the evolutionary costs and benefits of various reproductive strategies evident among multicellular organisms.

The ultimate biological goal of any organism is to pass on its genetic information. Organisms have evolved two primary pathways to achieve this, each with distinct evolutionary trade-offs: Asexual and Sexual reproduction.

Asexual vs. Sexual Reproduction

Strategy Mechanism Evolutionary Benefit Evolutionary Cost
Asexual A single parent produces genetically identical clones (e.g., budding in Hydra, vegetative propagation). Extremely fast. Requires no energy to find a mate. Excellent in a stable, predictable environment. No genetic diversity. If a new disease or climate shift occurs, the entire cloned population could be wiped out.
Sexual Two parents contribute genetic material (sperm and egg) to create a unique offspring. High genetic diversity. Provides the variation necessary for populations to adapt and survive environmental changes. Energy intensive. Organisms must find and attract mates, and only half of an individual's genes are passed on.

Quantity vs. Quality: r/K Selection Theory

Even within sexual reproduction, animals take drastically different approaches to ensuring offspring survival:

  • r-Selected Species (Quantity): Produce massive numbers of offspring but provide zero parental care. Most offspring will die, but sheer numbers guarantee a few survive. Example: A single female Northern Pike (fish) can lay up to 100,000 eggs in a Saskatchewan lake.
  • K-Selected Species (Quality): Produce very few offspring but invest massive amounts of energy and time into parental care, ensuring a high survival rate. Example: A Black Bear typically has 2 cubs and raises them for up to two years.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Trembling Aspen

The Trembling Aspen (Populus tremuloides), a dominant tree in the Saskatchewan parkland, is a master of asexual reproduction. While it can produce seeds, it primarily spreads through underground root systems that send up new stems (suckers). An entire grove of hundreds of trees might actually be a single, massive, genetically identical organism connected underground—a clonal colony. This strategy allows them to rapidly take over a landscape after a prairie fire.

4.6 Defense Mechanisms [BI30-OL2e]

Learning Objective: Explore the behavioural, structural, and physiological adaptations that enable organisms to defend themselves against threats, such as pathogens, predators, and disease.

The natural world is an endless arms race between predator and prey, host and pathogen. To survive, organisms have evolved an arsenal of defense mechanisms.

  • Structural Defenses: Physical barriers. The thick bark of an oak tree, the quills of a porcupine, or the waxy cuticle of a leaf that prevents bacteria from entering.
  • Behavioural Defenses: Actions taken to avoid harm. The Killdeer bird faking a broken wing to draw predators away from its nest, the herding behavior of pronghorn antelope, or the nocturnal feeding habits of many desert animals.
  • Physiological Defenses: Internal chemical responses. This includes complex immune systems that produce targeted antibodies against viruses, the venom of a rattlesnake, or plants that produce toxic alkaloids to deter herbivores.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Battle Against Wheat Rust

Defense mechanisms aren't just for animals. Wheat Rust is a devastating fungal pathogen that attacks the stems of wheat plants, draining their nutrients and destroying crop yields. For decades, researchers at the University of Saskatchewan's Crop Development Centre have been engaged in an evolutionary arms race against this fungus. They cross-breed different wheat varieties to introduce new physiological defenses—specific resistance genes—into the wheat's DNA, staying one step ahead of the mutating fungus to protect the provincial economy.
