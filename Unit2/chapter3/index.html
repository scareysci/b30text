Chapter 3: Biological Classification

Earth is home to an estimated 8.7 million species, yet we have named only about 1.2 million. How do we make sense of this staggering diversity? Classification is the process of grouping organisms based on shared characteristics. It is not just a scientific exercise; it is a human fundamental. In this chapter, we will explore how classification systems serve our needs, how Indigenous knowledge provides alternative ways of viewing relationships, and the modern tools biologists use to map the tree of life.

3.1 Human Design in Classification [BI30-OL3a]

Learning Objective: Discuss how classification systems are designed by humans to meet various needs, recognizing that these systems are tools rather than absolute truths.

Classification is a human construct. We organize the world to make it predictable and manageable. The way we group organisms often depends on our context and our needs.

  • Functional Classification: This is based on how we use an organism. A farmer might classify plants as "crops," "weeds," or "forage." This system isn't concerned with DNA; it's concerned with utility.
  • Morphological Classification: This groups organisms by physical appearance. Early naturalists grouped all flying animals (birds, bats, butterflies) together. We now know these animals aren't closely related, but the grouping was useful for describing movement.
  • Phylogenetic Classification: The modern scientific standard. It groups organisms based on evolutionary ancestry. This is the "true" biological relationship, often revealed through genetic sequencing.

Because our knowledge is always growing, classification systems must be dynamic. As we discover new species or sequence new genomes, we often have to "move the furniture" in our biological house, renaming or regrouping organisms to reflect the most current evidence.

Saskatchewan Connection: Agricultural Classification

In Saskatchewan, we use functional classification every day in the "Seeds Act." Plants are classified by their impact on the economy. For example, Wild Oats (Avena fatua) are biologically very similar to domestic oats, but in an agricultural context, they are classified as a "Noxious Weed" because they compete with high-value crops.

3.2 Indigenous Knowledge Systems [BI30-OL3b]

Learning Objective: Research how First Nations, Métis, and Inuit peoples represent their understandings of relationships among living things, and compare these to Linnaean systems.

For thousands of years before the invention of modern taxonomy, Indigenous peoples developed sophisticated systems for classifying the natural world. These systems are often holistic and relational.

Kinship and Connection

In many Indigenous worldviews, classification is not just about what an organism is, but who its relations are. This is often described through the concept of Wâhkôhtowin. Relationships are tracked not just through physical traits, but through ecological roles, seasonal timing, and spiritual significance.

3.3 Naming the Natural World [BI30-OL3c]

Learning Objective: Research the contributions of Carl Linnaeus in developing accepted scientific conventions for the naming of organisms.

In the 18th century, a Swedish botanist named Carl Linnaeus revolutionized biology by creating a standardized naming system. Before Linnaeus, a plant might have a name ten words long that changed from one town to the next.

Binomial Nomenclature

Linnaeus gave every species a two-part name (the "scientific name") using Latin:

  1. Genus: The first name, always capitalized (e.g., Homo). It represents a group of closely related species.
  2. Specific Epithet: The second name, always lowercase (e.g., sapiens). It identifies the specific species within that genus.

3.4 The Taxonomic Hierarchy [BI30-OL3d]

Learning Objective: Discuss how the characteristics of the taxonomic hierarchy represent relationships among organisms.

Taxonomy is organized like a set of nesting dolls. As you move from the top (Domain) to the bottom (Species), the groups become more specific and the members more similar.

3.5 Challenges in Classification [BI30-OL3e]

Learning Objective: Discuss the challenges associated with classification, including defining taxa and classifying certain organisms like protists.

Nature does not always respect our neat categories. Some organisms defy simple classification, leading to "Taxonomic Headaches."

  • The Protist Problem: Kingdom Protista is often called the "taxonomic junk drawer."
  • Horizontal Gene Transfer: Bacteria can swap DNA with unrelated species.
  • Hybrids: The biological species concept relies on reproductive isolation, which isn't always absolute.

3.6 Tools for Identification [BI30-OL3f, BI30-OL3g]

Learning Objective: Create and use dichotomous keys and cladograms to identify and determine relationships among organisms.

1. Dichotomous Keys

A dichotomous key is a step-by-step guide where you choose between two opposing physical traits until the organism is identified.

2. Cladograms

A cladogram is a diagram showing evolutionary relationships. It focuses on derived characters—traits that appeared in a common ancestor and were passed to descendants.
