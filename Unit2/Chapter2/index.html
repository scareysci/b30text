Unit 2: The Organization of Life

Unit Overview: Life is built on architecture. From the smallest bacterium to the largest blue whale, every living organism relies on the precise organization of matter to survive. In Unit 1, we defined what life is. In Unit 2, we explore how life is built. We will journey into the microscopic world of the cell—the fundamental unit of life—to understand the machinery that powers existence. We will examine how cells harvest energy, how they build the structures necessary for survival, and how they interact with their environment.

Saskatchewan Context: The Living Prairie

As we explore the organization of life, we acknowledge that we are learning on the traditional territories of the Nêhiyawak, Anihšināpēk, Dakota, Lakota, and Nakoda, and the homeland of the Métis/Michif Nation.

Indigenous knowledge teaches that all life is interconnected (wâhkôhtowin). Just as a single cell cannot survive without its organelles working in harmony, no organism exists in isolation from its environment. As you study the internal structures of the cell, consider how these microscopic systems reflect the larger systems of balance and reciprocity found in nature.

Chapter 2: Cell Structure and Processes

2.1 The Two Empires: Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

Learning Objective: Differentiate between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells based on their structure, complexity, and evolutionary history.

Before we examine the complex machinery of plants and animals, we must distinguish between the two fundamental types of cells that exist on Earth. This distinction is the most significant division in biology.

Prokaryotes ("Before Nucleus")

These are the simplest and most ancient forms of life. They lack membrane-bound organelles.

  • No Nucleus: Their DNA is free-floating in a region called the nucleoid.
  • Small & Simple: They are typically 10-100 times smaller than eukaryotic cells.
  • Examples: Bacteria (like E. coli) and Archaea (extremophiles).

Eukaryotes ("True Nucleus")

These cells evolved later and are much more complex. They contain internal membrane-bound compartments.

  • Nucleus: DNA is protected inside a double membrane.
  • Organelles: They have specialized "rooms" (mitochondria, chloroplasts, lysosomes) to perform specific tasks.
  • Examples: Plants, Animals, Fungi, and Protists (like Amoeba).
Feature Prokaryotes Eukaryotes
Nucleus No (Nucleoid) Yes
Membrane-Bound Organelles No Yes (Mitochondria, Golgi, etc.)
Size Small (1-5 $\mu m$) Large (10-100 $\mu m$)
DNA Structure Circular Loop Linear Chromosomes

Saskatchewan Connection: Pulse Crops and Nitrogen Fixation

Saskatchewan is a world leader in lentil and pea production. These plants rely on a symbiotic relationship with Rhizobium, a genus of soil bacteria (prokaryotes). The bacteria live in nodules on the plant roots and convert atmospheric nitrogen into a form the plant can use—a feat no eukaryotic cell can perform on its own.

2.2 Reviewing the Foundation: Plant and Animal Cell Structures

Learning Objective: Review and compare the major organelles of plant and animal cells, establishing a foundation for understanding complex cellular processes.

Now that we have distinguished eukaryotes from prokaryotes, we will focus on the two main types of eukaryotic cells relevant to this course: Plant and Animal cells. You are likely familiar with the basic model, but in Biology 30, we move beyond simple identification to understanding the functional integration of these structures.

Eukaryotic cells are defined by their compartmentalization. Internal membranes create enclosed spaces called organelles, each with a specific chemical environment. This allows incompatible processes—like the creation of proteins and the digestion of waste—to occur simultaneously without interference.

Defining the Boundaries

  • The Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane): Found in both plant and animal cells. It is a flexible, selective barrier that acts as the gatekeeper, regulating the passage of nutrients, water, and waste.
  • The Cell Wall: Found only in plant cells (in this comparison). This is a rigid, non-living outer layer made of cellulose. It sits outside the cell membrane and provides the structural support necessary for plants to grow upright against gravity. The outermost layer that cements adjacent plant cells together is called the middle lamella.

The Endomembrane System

Key to this organization is the endomembrane system.

  • The Nucleus: The command center housing the genetic blueprint (DNA). It is surrounded by a double membrane (nuclear envelope) with pores that strictly regulate traffic.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):
    • Rough ER: Studded with ribosomes; this is the factory for protein synthesis. Ribosomes are the specific sites where amino acids are assembled into proteins.
    • Smooth ER: Synthesizes lipids and detoxifies poisons.
  • Golgi Apparatus (Golgi Complex): The post office of the cell. It modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for transport.
  • Lysosomes: Found primarily in animal cells, these contain digestive enzymes to break down waste or food.
    Fungal Note: Fungi do not ingest food like animals. Instead, they excrete digestive enzymes outside their cells to degrade the substrate (extracellular digestion) and then absorb the nutrients.
  • Vesicles: Small membrane sacs that act as transport vehicles.

Powerhouses and Motion

  • Mitochondria: Found in both plant and animal cells, they perform cellular respiration, converting chemical energy (glucose) into usable energy (ATP).
  • Chloroplasts: Found only in plants and some protists, they are the sites of photosynthesis, converting solar energy into chemical energy.
  • Centrioles: Short, tube-like organelles found in animal cells (but not most plants) that help organize cell division (mitosis).
  • Cilia and Flagella: Hair-like extensions used for movement.
    • Cilia: Short and numerous (like oars).
    • Flagella: Long and few (like a whip). Both use ATP to power their movement.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Canola Cell

Consider the cells of a Canola plant (Brassica napus), one of Saskatchewan's most important crops. Its cells are packed with chloroplasts to maximize the short growing season. Furthermore, the seeds of the canola plant contain specialized organelles called oleosomes (oil bodies), which store the valuable lipids that we harvest as canola oil.

2.3 Energy Creation and Transfer: The Biochemical Pathways

Learning Objective: Analyze the specific biochemical pathways—Cellular Respiration and Photosynthesis—by which organisms create and transfer energy in the form of ATP.

Energy is the capacity to cause change. In biological systems, this energy must be converted into a usable form: Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP). While the overall equations for respiration and photosynthesis seem simple, the actual processes involve complex, multi-step pathways that rely on precise machinery within cellular organelles.

The Structure of ATP

ATP is often compared to a fully charged battery. It consists of three parts: Adenine (nitrogenous base), Ribose (sugar), and a chain of three Phosphate Groups.

The key to ATP's power lies in the phosphate tail. These groups are negatively charged and repel each other, like a compressed spring. When the third phosphate bond is broken (hydrolysis), energy is released.

$$ \text{ATP} + \mathrm{H_2O} \rightarrow \text{ADP} + \text{P}_i + \text{Energy} $$

Part A: Cellular Respiration (The Spender)

This process occurs in the mitochondria of all eukaryotic organisms. It breaks down glucose to produce ATP.

$$ \mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6} + 6\mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow 6\mathrm{CO_2} + 6\mathrm{H_2O} + 36\text{-}38 \text{ ATP} $$
1. Glycolysis ("Sugar Breaking")

Occurs in the Cytoplasm (Cytosol). Glucose splits into two Pyruvate molecules. Anaerobic (does not need oxygen). Yields a net of 2 ATP and NADH.

2. The Transition Reaction (Intermediate Step)

Pyruvate enters the mitochondria and is converted into Acetyl-CoA. $\mathrm{CO_2}$ is released.

3. Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

Occurs in the Mitochondrial Matrix. Acetyl-CoA is stripped of carbon (released as $\mathrm{CO_2}$). Yields 2 ATP and fully loaded electron carriers (NADH, $\mathrm{FADH_2}$). This is where the most $\mathrm{CO_2}$ is produced.

4. Oxidative Phosphorylation (Electron Transport Chain)

Occurs in the Inner Membrane (Cristae). High-energy electrons pump $\mathrm{H^+}$ ions, creating a gradient. $\mathrm{H^+}$ rushes back through ATP Synthase to generate approx. 32-34 ATP (the most of any stage). Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water ($\mathrm{H_2O}$).

Key Concept: Aerobic vs. Anaerobic

Cells prefer aerobic respiration because it produces ~36-38 ATP per glucose. Without oxygen (anaerobic), they are stuck doing only Glycolysis, producing only 2 ATP. Complex life requires the high efficiency of aerobic respiration.

Scientific Tracking

Scientists used radioactive oxygen ($\mathrm{^{18}O}$) to trace metabolic paths. If cells are supplied with radioactive oxygen gas ($\mathrm{O_2}$), the radioactivity ends up in the Water ($\mathrm{H_2O}$) produced at the end of the Electron Transport Chain.

Part B: Photosynthesis (The Builder)

Occurring in chloroplasts, this uses light to build glucose.

$$ 6\mathrm{CO_2} + 6\mathrm{H_2O} + \text{Light} \rightarrow \mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6} + 6\mathrm{O_2} $$
1. Light-Dependent Reactions

Occurs in Thylakoid Membranes (Grana). Sunlight hits chlorophyll photosystems. Photolysis splits water: Hydrogen is kept for the ETC, and Oxygen ($\mathrm{O_2}$) is released as waste. Electron transport generates ATP and NADPH.

2. Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle)

Occurs in the Stroma. $\mathrm{CO_2}$ enters the cycle.

  • Carbon Fixation: An enzyme called Rubisco attaches $\mathrm{CO_2}$ to a 5-carbon sugar called RuBP.
  • Reduction: Energy from ATP and NADPH converts the molecule into G3P.
  • Regeneration: Some G3P leaves to become Glucose; the rest is recycled back into RuBP to keep the cycle going.

Saskatchewan Connection: Solar Efficiency in the Wheat Belt

Saskatchewan is the "breadbasket of Canada." The efficiency of these Light-Dependent and Light-Independent Reactions in wheat varieties developed at the University of Saskatchewan directly impacts our economy. Researchers study how to maximize the capture of solar energy during our long summer days to produce starch-rich kernels.

2.4 The Molecules of Life

Learning Objective: Describe how biologically important molecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) work together to form complex cellular structures.

Cells are not random collections of atoms; they are complex structures built from macromolecules. These four major classes serve as the energy, blueprint, and bricks and mortar of cellular architecture.

1. Carbohydrates: Energy and Structure

Carbohydrates are the cell's primary energy source and structural material. They are made of Carbon, Hydrogen, and Oxygen, usually in a 1:2:1 ratio ($\mathrm{CH_2O}$).

  • Simple Carbohydrates (Monosaccharides): The fundamental building blocks. Glucose ($\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}$) is the most critical fuel for cellular respiration.
  • Complex Carbohydrates (Polysaccharides): Long chains of glucose units linked together.
    • Starch: Energy storage in plants (e.g., potatoes).
    • Glycogen: Energy storage in animals (stored in your liver and muscles).
    • Cellulose: Structural support in plant cell walls (fiber).

2. Lipids: The Architects of Boundaries

Lipids are hydrophobic (water-fearing) molecules. While they store long-term energy (fats), their most crucial role in the cell is building the membrane.

The Phospholipid

The cell membrane is built primarily of phospholipids. Each molecule is distinct, having two parts with opposite chemical properties (amphipathic):

  • Hydrophilic Head: Contains a phosphate group; loves water ("hydro" = water, "philic" = loving).
  • Hydrophobic Tails: Made of fatty acids; hate water ("phobic" = fearing).
The Fluid Mosaic Model

In water, phospholipids spontaneously arrange into a bilayer, with heads facing the water inside and outside the cell, and tails hiding in the middle. Proteins float in this sea of lipids like icebergs, creating a "fluid mosaic."

3. Proteins: The Cellular Machinery

Proteins are the workhorses of life. They do almost everything: catalyze reactions (enzymes), transport molecules (hemoglobin), and provide structure (keratin).

Building Blocks

Proteins are polymers made of Amino Acids. There are 20 different amino acids, linked together by peptide bonds to form long chains (polypeptides). The sequence of amino acids determines the protein's shape, and the shape determines its function.

Levels of Protein Structure
  • Primary Structure: The linear sequence of amino acids (like beads on a string).
  • Secondary Structure: Local folding into alpha-helices (coils) and beta-sheets (pleats) due to hydrogen bonding.
  • Tertiary Structure: The comprehensive 3D folding of the entire chain.
  • Quaternary Structure: When two or more polypeptide chains clump together to form a functional unit (e.g., Hemoglobin has 4 subunits).

4. Nucleic Acids: The Information Storage

Nucleic acids store and transmit hereditary information. There are two types: DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) and RNA (Ribonucleic Acid).

Feature DNA RNA
Sugar Deoxyribose Ribose
Strands Double Helix Single Strand
Nitrogen Bases A, C, G, Thymine (T) A, C, G, Uracil (U)

The Double Helix: DNA consists of two strands twisting around each other. The "rungs" of the ladder are nitrogen bases held together by weak Hydrogen Bonds. Adenine always bonds with Thymine (A-T), and Cytosine always bonds with Guanine (C-G). This specific pairing is the secret to how DNA copies itself.

Saskatchewan Connection: Flax and Omega-3s

Saskatchewan produces a significant portion of the world's flax. Flax seeds are rich in Omega-3 fatty acids—lipids essential for building flexible, healthy cell membranes in the human body.

2.5 Cell Walls Across Kingdoms

Learning Objective: Compare the structure and composition of cell walls in plants, fungi, and bacteria.

The cell wall provides structural integrity, but its composition varies across kingdoms—a classic example of convergent evolution.

Kingdom Cell Wall Component Properties
Plants Cellulose Tough fibers (polysaccharide). Acts like steel cables. Includes the middle lamella, which cements cells together.
Fungi Chitin Strong and flexible nitrogen-containing polysaccharide. Also found in insect exoskeletons.
Bacteria Peptidoglycan Mesh of sugars and amino acids. Targeted by antibiotics like Penicillin.
Gram Staining

Bacteria are often classified by their cell wall structure using the Gram Stain technique:

  • Gram Positive: Have a thick peptidoglycan wall that retains the purple stain.
  • Gram Negative: Have a thinner wall and an extra outer membrane, causing them to appear pink.

Saskatchewan Connection: Clubroot Disease

Clubroot is a devastating disease affecting Saskatchewan canola caused by a protist. Understanding the cell wall composition of both the crop (cellulose) and the pathogen is crucial for research.

2.6 The Versatility of Unicellular Organisms

Learning Objective: Examine how unicellular organisms exhibit all characteristics of living things within a single cell.

A single cell must find food, digest it, release energy, move, expel waste, and reproduce. The Paramecium is a perfect case study.

  • Nutrition: Cilia sweep bacteria into an oral groove.
  • Digestion: Food vacuoles fuse with lysosomes.
  • Homeostasis: The Contractile Vacuole pumps excess water out to prevent bursting in fresh water.
Amoeboid Movement

Amoebas move by extending their cytoplasm into Pseudopods ("false feet"). They anchor the pseudopod and pull the rest of the cell forward. This same mechanism allows them to engulf food (phagocytosis).

Cellular Interactions (Symbiosis)

Organisms often live in close association with one another. These relationships are called symbiosis:

  • Mutualism: Both benefit (e.g., termites and the microbes in their gut that digest wood).
  • Commensalism: One benefits, the other is unaffected.
  • Parasitism: One benefits, the other is harmed.
  • Amensalism: One is harmed, the other is unaffected (e.g., Penicillium mold secreting antibiotics that kill bacteria).

Saskatchewan Connection: Sloughs and Ponds

Scoop water from any prairie slough, and you will find a metropolis of unicellular life like Daphnia and Paramecia, forming the base of the food web.

2.7 Life in the Extreme: Archaeans

Learning Objective: Research how structural and biochemical adaptations enable archaeans to live in extreme environments.

Archaea are extremophiles. They survive using ether-linked lipids (stable in heat/acid) and specialized stable enzymes (like heat-resistant DNA polymerase).

  • Thermophiles: Heat lovers.
  • Halophiles: Salt lovers.
  • Methanogens: Oxygen haters (produce methane as waste).

Saskatchewan Connection: Chaplin Lake

Chaplin Lake is a saline lake in southern Saskatchewan. Halophilic Archaea thrive here, producing red pigments that can turn the water pink.

2.8 Tools of the Trade

Learning Objective: Research advancements in microscopy and how they have deepened our understanding of cell structure.
  1. Compound Light Microscope: Uses light. Good for live cells. Low mag (~1000x).
  2. Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM): Electrons pass through. Sees internal details. Specimen must be dead. High mag.
  3. Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM): Electrons bounce off surface. 3D images. Specimen must be dead.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Canadian Light Source

The CLS in Saskatoon is a synchrotron used to analyze chemical compositions at the molecular level.

2.9 Crossing the Border: Membrane Transport

Learning Objective: Demonstrate transportation of materials through active and passive transport and explain the role of osmosis.
Passive Transport (No Energy)

Driven by concentration gradient (High → Low). No ATP required.

  • Diffusion: Movement of small particles (like oxygen) directly through the membrane.
  • Facilitated Diffusion: Use of protein channels for larger items.
  • Osmosis: Diffusion of water.
Active Transport (ATP Required)

Pumps materials against the gradient (Low → High) or moves massive items.

  • Protein Pumps: E.g., Sodium-Potassium pump.
  • Endocytosis: Taking bulky material in using vesicles.
    • Phagocytosis: "Cell eating" (solids).
    • Pinocytosis: "Cell drinking" (liquids/droplets).
  • Exocytosis: Expelling material out using vesicles.

Saskatchewan Connection: Fertilizer Burn

Too much nitrogen fertilizer creates a hypertonic environment in the soil, drawing water out of plant roots via osmosis, "burning" the crop.
