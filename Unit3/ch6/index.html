6.1 The Race for the Double Helix [BI30-GB2a, b, c]

Learning Objective: Research the scientific culture surrounding the discovery of DNA's structure and assess how this double helix structure allows DNA to store, transmit, and express information.

The discovery of the DNA molecule's structure is arguably the most transformative event in the history of biology. However, it was not the work of a lone genius having a "eureka" moment. It was a heated, competitive race during the 1950s, characterized by brilliant insights, clashing egos, and ethical controversies.

The Scientific Culture of the 1950s

Post-World War II science was heavily male-dominated and highly competitive. While we often celebrate collaborative science today, the race for DNA was driven by rival laboratories in England and the United States racing to publish first. Several key figures contributed pieces to the puzzle:

  • Erwin Chargaff: A biochemist who discovered a crucial chemical rule. He found that in any sample of DNA, the amount of Adenine (A) always equalled the amount of Thymine (T), and the amount of Cytosine (C) always equalled Guanine (G). This became known as Chargaff's Rules, hinting that these bases must pair up.
  • Rosalind Franklin & Maurice Wilkins: Working at King's College London, they used X-ray crystallography to take pictures of DNA molecules. Franklin, a brilliant and meticulous chemist, produced "Photo 51"—an X-ray diffraction image that clearly showed DNA possessed a helical (corkscrew) shape.
  • James Watson & Francis Crick: Working at Cambridge University, these two were theoretical model builders. They were struggling to figure out how the chemical pieces fit together.
The Ethical Controversy of Photo 51

Without Rosalind Franklin's knowledge or permission, Maurice Wilkins showed Watson her unpublished "Photo 51". The moment Watson saw the distinct 'X' pattern in the X-ray image, he knew the molecule was a double helix. Watson and Crick used her data to build their famous 1953 model and eventually won the Nobel Prize. Franklin passed away from ovarian cancer before the prize was awarded (Nobel prizes are not awarded posthumously), and her critical contribution was largely overlooked for decades.

The Elegance of the Double Helix

Why was discovering the structure so important? Because in biology, structure dictates function. The moment Watson and Crick built the double helix model, it instantly explained how DNA could copy itself.

DNA is a polymer made of repeating units called nucleotides. Each nucleotide has three parts: a deoxyribose sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (A, T, C, or G).

  • Storage: The sequence of the bases (A, T, C, G) along the center acts like letters in a book. This specific sequence is the genetic code.
  • Transmission (Copying): The two sides are held together by weak hydrogen bonds. Like a zipper, the DNA can easily be pulled apart down the middle. Because A only pairs with T, and C only pairs with G, each separated half serves as a perfect template to build a new, identical DNA molecule.
  • Protection: The outside of the helix is a tough "sugar-phosphate backbone" held together by strong covalent bonds, protecting the fragile genetic code inside from chemical damage.

DNA vs. RNA: The Two Nucleic Acids

While DNA is the famous master blueprint of life, it doesn't work alone. It relies on a closely related cousin molecule called RNA (Ribonucleic Acid). While we will see RNA in action in the next section, it is crucial to understand the structural differences between these two nucleic acids now.

Feature DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) RNA (Ribonucleic Acid)
Sugar Deoxyribose (missing one oxygen atom) Ribose
Structure Double-stranded (Double Helix) Usually Single-stranded
Nitrogenous Bases Adenine (A), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G), Thymine (T) Adenine (A), Cytosine (C), Guanine (G), Uracil (U)
Primary Function Long-term storage of genetic information. Short-term transmission of information and protein synthesis.
Location Permanently trapped inside the Nucleus (in eukaryotes). Can move freely from the Nucleus to the Cytoplasm.

6.2 From DNA to Protein: The Central Dogma [BI30-GB2d]

Learning Objective: Model the molecular genetic processes of DNA replication and protein synthesis (transcription and translation), distinguishing the specific roles of DNA, mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA.

DNA contains the instructions, but DNA doesn't actually do the physical work in your body. Proteins do. Proteins build muscle, digest food, carry oxygen, and fight viruses. The process of converting the written DNA code into a physical, working 3D protein is called the Central Dogma of Molecular Biology. It flows in one direction: DNA → RNA → Protein.

Step 1: DNA Replication (Copying the Manual)

Before a cell can divide, it must copy its entire DNA so the new cell has a manual. This process is called semi-conservative replication because each new DNA molecule keeps one "old" strand and builds one "new" strand.

  1. An enzyme called Helicase unzips the double helix by breaking the weak hydrogen bonds between the bases.
  2. An enzyme called DNA Polymerase reads the exposed bases and attaches free-floating, matching nucleotides (A with T, C with G) to build the new complementary strand.

Step 2: Transcription (Writing the Message)

Your DNA is a master blueprint permanently locked inside the nucleus for protection. To build a protein, the cell needs to send a temporary copy of a specific gene out to the cytoplasm. This copy is made of mRNA (messenger RNA).

  • An enzyme called RNA Polymerase binds to the gene, opens the DNA, and builds a single-stranded mRNA copy.
  • Crucial Difference: RNA does not contain the base Thymine (T). Instead, it uses Uracil (U). So, if the DNA reads 'A', the mRNA will add a 'U'.

Step 3: Translation (Building the Machine)

The mRNA leaves the nucleus and enters the cytoplasm, where it finds a Ribosome. The ribosome is a molecular factory made primarily of rRNA (ribosomal RNA). Here, the language of nucleic acids is translated into the language of amino acids.

The ribosome reads the mRNA sequence three letters at a time. Every three letters is called a Codon, and it codes for one specific amino acid. For example, the mRNA sequence AUG is the "Start" codon and codes for the amino acid Methionine.

To bring the correct amino acids to the factory, the cell uses a delivery service: tRNA (transfer RNA). Each tRNA carries a specific amino acid on one end, and has an anti-codon on the other end that perfectly matches the mRNA codon. As the ribosome reads the mRNA, tRNA molecules drop off their amino acids, which link together to form a long polypeptide chain. When the ribosome hits a "Stop" codon, the chain is released, folds into a 3D shape, and becomes a functional protein!

6.3 Mutations and Repair [BI30-GB2e, f, g]

Learning Objective: Discuss types of point mutations (substitution, insertion, deletion) and their effects (missense, nonsense, neutrality/silent) including reading frameshifts. Assess the value of DNA proofreading and the role of mutation in evolution.

DNA replication is incredibly fast—up to 50 nucleotides per second in human cells. With 3 billion base pairs to copy, mistakes happen. A permanent change in the DNA sequence is called a mutation. While radiation and toxic chemicals (mutagens) can cause mutations, many are simply natural "typos" made by DNA Polymerase.

Point Mutations (Substitutions)

A point mutation occurs when a single nucleotide is swapped for another (e.g., an 'A' is accidentally swapped for a 'C'). Because the genetic code is read in three-letter codons, this substitution only affects one single amino acid. The effect depends on where it happens:

  • Neutrality (Silent Mutation): The mutation changes a base, but because the genetic code has redundancies, the new codon still codes for the exact same amino acid. The protein functions perfectly. The mutation is silent.
  • Missense Mutation: The substitution changes the codon to code for a different amino acid. The resulting protein might work slightly differently, barely work at all, or in rare cases, work better. (Sickle Cell Anemia is caused by a single missense mutation).
  • Nonsense Mutation: The substitution accidentally changes an amino acid codon into a "STOP" codon. The ribosome stops building prematurely. The protein is cut short and is almost always completely non-functional.

Frameshift Mutations (Insertions and Deletions)

These are often much more devastating. If DNA Polymerase accidentally inserts an extra nucleotide, or deletes a nucleotide, it disrupts the entire reading frame.

Consider this English sentence of three-letter words:

THE FAT CAT ATE THE RAT

If we Delete the 'C' in CAT, the ribosome still reads in threes, shifting everything over:

THE FAT ATA TET HER AT

The entire sentence (protein) after the deletion is completely ruined and unrecognizable.

DNA Proofreading and Evolution

If mutations are so dangerous, how do we survive? DNA Polymerase isn't just a builder; it's also a proofreader. It checks its work as it goes. If it adds the wrong base, it can back up, cut the mistake out, and try again. This proofreading prevents 99% of mutations. Other specialized repair enzymes constantly patrol the DNA looking for damage from UV light to fix.

However, a perfect repair system would mean no mutations ever survive. Mutations are the raw material of evolution. Without these random genetic variations, natural selection would have no new traits to "select" from. A mutation might break a protein today, but in a changing environment tomorrow, that broken protein might accidentally provide a survival advantage.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Kochia Weed Arms Race

Saskatchewan farmers rely on herbicides to control weeds. However, Kochia (a tumbleweed-like plant) has become a massive agricultural nightmare. How? A random missense point mutation occurred in the DNA of a few Kochia plants, slightly altering the shape of a specific protein targeted by herbicides. The herbicide could no longer bind to the mutant protein. The weed survived, reproduced, and passed on the mutation. In the environment of a sprayed field, this random "typo" was a brilliant evolutionary advantage, leading to herbicide-resistant "superweeds" across the prairies.

6.4 Nature vs. Nurture: Epigenetics [BI30-GB2h]

Learning Objective: Describe how environmental and epigenetic factors influence genetic expression without changing the underlying DNA sequence.

For decades, scientists believed that your traits were completely locked in by the DNA sequence you inherited from your parents. You were a product of your genes ("Nature"). But what about identical twins who share the exact same DNA, yet one develops a disease and the other doesn't? The answer lies in Epigenetics (meaning "above" the genome).

Your DNA is hardware. Epigenetics is the software that tells the hardware what to do. Every cell in your body has the exact same DNA, but a heart cell looks and acts completely different from a skin cell. This is because cells can turn specific genes "ON" or "OFF."

How Epigenetics Works

The environment (diet, stress, toxins, exercise) can cause chemical tags to attach to your DNA or the proteins that wrap it (histones).

  • DNA Methylation: The most common tag is a methyl group. When methyl groups attach to a gene, they physically block RNA Polymerase. The gene is silenced (turned OFF) and cannot be transcribed into mRNA.
  • These epigenetic tags can change throughout your life based on your lifestyle and environment ("Nurture").

Astonishingly, recent research shows that these epigenetic tags can sometimes be inherited by the next generation. If an organism experiences severe trauma or starvation, the epigenetic changes designed to help it survive can be passed to its offspring, preparing the offspring's biology for a harsh environment before they are even born.

Intergenerational Trauma and Epigenetics

Indigenous Knowledge Systems have long maintained that the experiences of the ancestors live within the present generations. A core aspect of Wâhkôhtowin is understanding that our physical and spiritual health is deeply connected to the generations before us and those yet to come.

Western molecular biology is now finding empirical evidence for this reality through epigenetics. Groundbreaking research is demonstrating that historical traumas—such as the starvation policies enacted on the Great Plains in the 1880s, or the systemic stress, abuse, and cultural suppression of the Residential School system—caused profound physiological stress in Indigenous ancestors. This extreme stress altered their epigenetic markers, particularly those regulating the stress hormone cortisol.

These altered epigenetic tags (turning certain stress-response genes permanently "on" or "off") can be biologically inherited by subsequent generations. This means that a grandchild of a Residential School survivor may be born with a biological predisposition to heightened anxiety or altered metabolic responses, even if they never experienced the original trauma themselves. Understanding epigenetics bridges Western biological science with Indigenous historical experiences, highlighting the deep, molecular impact of colonialism and emphasizing the biological importance of community healing.

6.5 The Molecular Toolbox [BI30-GB2i]

Learning Objective: Model or simulate common techniques used by molecular geneticists, such as PCR, agarose gel electrophoresis, and DNA fingerprinting.

Understanding DNA is one thing; manipulating it is another. Over the last forty years, scientists have developed a "toolbox" of biochemical techniques that allow us to cut, copy, sort, and read DNA. These tools are the foundation of modern forensics, medicine, and agriculture.

1. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

Imagine finding a tiny speck of blood at a crime scene. It contains DNA, but not nearly enough to analyze. PCR is a molecular photocopier. By utilizing a heat-resistant DNA Polymerase enzyme (discovered in extreme thermophilic bacteria!), scientists can force DNA to replicate in a test tube. By rapidly heating and cooling the sample, a single copy of DNA can be amplified into billions of copies in just a few hours.

2. Restriction Enzymes (Molecular Scissors)

To analyze DNA, we often need to cut it into smaller pieces. Scientists use Restriction Enzymes (originally discovered in bacteria as a defense against viruses). These enzymes scan the DNA and cut the strands only at very specific letter sequences (e.g., every time they see GAATTC).

3. Agarose Gel Electrophoresis (Sorting the Pieces)

Once DNA is cut into fragments, how do we see them? We sort them by size using Gel Electrophoresis.

  1. The chopped-up DNA mixture is loaded into small wells inside a slab of agarose gel (which acts like a microscopic sponge or sieve).
  2. An electrical current is turned on. Because the phosphate backbone of DNA is negatively charged, the DNA fragments are pulled through the gel towards the positive electrical pole.
  3. The Sorting: Small, short pieces of DNA slip through the gel's pores easily and travel very fast. Large, bulky pieces of DNA get tangled in the gel and travel very slowly.
  4. After an hour, the current is turned off. The DNA is now separated by size into visible "bands."

4. DNA Fingerprinting

While 99.9% of human DNA is identical, we all have regions of "junk DNA" that vary wildly in length between individuals. If we use restriction enzymes to cut a suspect's DNA around these varying regions, and then run it through a gel, we get a unique barcode-like pattern of bands. If the suspect's barcode perfectly matches the barcode from the crime scene blood, it's a definitive match.
