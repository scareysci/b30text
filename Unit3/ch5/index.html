Chapter 5: Biological Inheritance

Have you ever wondered why you might have your mother's eyes but your grandfather's nose? Or why two brown-eyed parents can have a blue-eyed child? For millennia, humans recognized that traits were passed down from generation to generation, but the mechanisms of this inheritance were a mystery. Farmers bred the fastest horses and the hardiest wheat, relying on the visible outcomes of biology without understanding the invisible code driving it.

In this chapter, we will decode that mystery. We will transition from the cellular structures studied in Unit 2 to the profound implications of the information stored inside the nucleus. By exploring classical (Mendelian) genetics and current chromosomal understandings, we will learn how probability, specific patterns of inheritance, and the microscopic dance of meiosis ensure both the continuity of a species and the brilliant diversity of life.

5.1 Tracing History: The Inheritance of Traits [BI30-GB1a, BI30-GB1b, BI30-GB1d]

Learning Objective: Explore patterns of inheritance by interpreting pedigrees and discussing the importance of probability in predicting the likelihood of traits.

The study of genetics is fundamentally the study of probability. When parents reproduce sexually, they do not pass on their exact genetic makeup. Instead, they pass on a randomized half of their genetic information. Because of this, we cannot say with absolute certainty what a child will look like; we can only predict the likelihood of inheriting particular traits.

Interpreting Pedigrees

To understand probability, biologists must look to the past. A pedigree is a genetic family tree that visually maps the occurrence of a specific trait through multiple generations of a family. By analyzing these patterns, geneticists can determine whether a trait is dominant, recessive, or linked to a sex chromosome.

  • Squares represent males.
  • Circles represent females.
  • Shaded shapes indicate the individual expresses the trait being studied.
  • Horizontal lines connecting a male and female indicate a mating pair.
  • Vertical lines dropping down from a pair lead to their offspring.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Black Angus Pedigree

Applied pedigree analysis is crucial to Saskatchewan's agricultural economy. Consider the Black Angus cattle breed, highly prized by provincial ranchers. Coat color in cattle is genetically determined. Black coat color is a dominant trait, while a red coat is recessive. A rancher wanting to maintain a pure Black Angus herd must carefully map the pedigrees of their bulls and cows to ensure they are not unknowingly harboring the hidden (recessive) gene for the red coat, which could pop up unexpectedly in future generations.

5.2 Decoding the Organism: Mendelian Genetics [BI30-GB1c, e, f, g]

Learning Objective: Discuss the historical development of Mendelian genetics, determine phenotypes from genotypes, and use Punnett squares to predict offspring frequencies across different patterns of inheritance.

The mathematical foundation of genetics was not laid by a modern scientist with a high-tech lab, but by an Austrian monk named Gregor Mendel in the 1860s. Mendel is considered the "father of genetics" because he was the first to use rigorous statistical analysis to track inheritance, spending years cultivating over 28,000 pea plants.

Why Peas? The Genius of Mendel's Model

Mendel’s success was largely due to his careful selection of the garden pea (Pisum sativum) as his model organism. He chose them for several crucial reasons:

  • Controlled Mating: Pea plants naturally self-pollinate, but Mendel could easily open the petals, remove the pollen-producing anthers, and manually cross-pollinate them with a specific partner using a small paintbrush.
  • Distinct, Observable Traits: He selected varieties that differed in stark, "either/or" ways—there were no "medium" height plants, only tall or short. There were no yellowish-green peas, only yellow or green.
  • Rapid Reproduction: They grow quickly, allowing him to observe multiple generations in a short time.
  • High Yield: Each plant produces many peas (offspring), providing the large sample sizes necessary to see clear mathematical ratios in his data.

Genotype vs. Phenotype

Mendel deduced that traits are controlled by discrete "factors" (which we now call genes). Every organism has two versions of each gene—one inherited from the mother, one from the father. These different versions of a gene are called alleles.

  • Genotype: The actual genetic makeup of the organism (the invisible code, represented by letters).
    • Homozygous Dominant (TT): Two dominant alleles.
    • Heterozygous (Tt): One dominant, one recessive allele.
    • Homozygous Recessive (tt): Two recessive alleles.
  • Phenotype: The physical, observable expression of that trait (what you see, e.g., "Tall" or "Short").

Predicting Offspring: The P, F1, and F2 Generations

To calculate the probability of offspring inheriting specific traits, biologists use a mathematical grid called a Punnett square. When tracking a trait across multiple generations (a monohybrid cross), geneticists use specific shorthand:

  • P Generation (Parental): The original true-breeding (homozygous) parents.
  • F1 Generation (First Filial): The offspring of the P generation cross.
  • F2 Generation (Second Filial): The offspring produced when two F1 individuals are crossed.

Let's walk through Mendel's classic experiment with flower color, crossing a pure-breeding purple flowered plant with a pure-breeding white flowered plant.

Step 1: The P Cross (PP x pp)

We cross a Homozygous Dominant purple plant (PP) with a Homozygous Recessive white plant (pp).

p (Sperm) p (Sperm)
P (Egg) Pp (Purple) Pp (Purple)
P (Egg) Pp (Purple) Pp (Purple)

F1 Results: 100% Heterozygous (Pp). Phenotype: 100% Purple. The white trait seems to have disappeared entirely!

Step 2: The F1 Cross (Pp x Pp)

Mendel then allowed the F1 generation to self-pollinate, crossing two Heterozygous purple plants (Pp x Pp).

P (Sperm) p (Sperm)
P (Egg) PP (Purple) Pp (Purple)
p (Egg) Pp (Purple) pp (White)

F2 Results: The Genotypic ratio is 1 PP : 2 Pp : 1 pp (1:2:1). The Phenotypic ratio is 3 Purple : 1 White (75% probability of a purple plant). The hidden white trait reappears!

Beyond Mendel: Complex Patterns of Inheritance

Mendel was lucky; pea plant traits are largely binary (tall/short, green/yellow). Nature is rarely that simple. Modern genetics recognizes multiple complex patterns:

  • Incomplete Dominance: Neither allele is completely dominant. A red flower and a white flower cross to produce a pink flower (a blending of traits).
  • Codominance: Both alleles are equally and fully expressed. A black chicken and a white chicken cross to produce a chicken with checkered black and white feathers.
  • Multiple Alleles: A gene that has more than two possible alleles in the population. Human blood type is the classic example (A, B, and O alleles combine to make types A, B, AB, or O).
  • Sex-Linked Traits: Genes located on the sex chromosomes (usually the X chromosome). Because males have only one X chromosome (XY), they are much more susceptible to recessive X-linked conditions like color blindness or hemophilia than females (XX).
  • Polygenic Inheritance: A single trait controlled by the interaction of many genes. Human height, skin color, and eye color are polygenic, resulting in a smooth continuum of phenotypes rather than discrete categories.

Mendelian Genetics in Humans

While humans are incredibly complex and many of our defining characteristics are polygenic, there are several physical traits that are traditionally modeled using simple Mendelian inheritance—meaning they are controlled by a single gene exhibiting complete dominance. Take a look at the traits below. Which phenotypes do you express?

Earlobe Attachment (E/e)

Dominant (E): Free-hanging earlobes.
Recessive (e): Attached earlobes.

Inheritance Mode: Complete Dominance. Individuals with EE or Ee genotypes will have earlobes that hang completely free at the bottom. Only individuals with the homozygous recessive ee genotype have earlobes that attach flush to the side of the head.

Hairline Shape (W/w)

Dominant (W): Widow's Peak (V-shaped point).
Recessive (w): Straight hairline.

Inheritance Mode: Complete Dominance. If you inherit at least one dominant W allele (WW or Ww), your hairline will form a distinct downward point at the center of your forehead. A straight hairline requires the ww genotype.

Thumb Mobility (H/h)

Dominant (H): Straight thumb.
Recessive (h): "Hitchhiker's" thumb.

Inheritance Mode: Complete Dominance. Give a thumbs up! If the upper joint of your thumb bends backward past a 50-degree angle, you express the recessive "hitchhiker's" phenotype (hh). If it remains relatively straight, you possess at least one dominant allele (HH or Hh).

Cleft Chin (C/c)

Dominant (C): Cleft/Dimpled chin.
Recessive (c): Smooth chin.

Inheritance Mode: Complete Dominance. A prominent dimple or Y-shaped fissure on the chin is a dominant trait. You only need one C allele to express it. A perfectly smooth, rounded chin requires the homozygous recessive cc genotype.

PTC Tasting (T/t)

Dominant (T): Can taste PTC (Bitter).
Recessive (t): Cannot taste PTC (Tasteless).

Inheritance Mode: Complete Dominance. Phenylthiocarbamide (PTC) is a harmless chemical used in genetics labs. For individuals with the TT or Tt genotypes, a strip of paper laced with PTC tastes incredibly bitter. For those with the homozygous recessive tt genotype, the paper tastes like nothing at all!

Saskatchewan Connection: Polygenic Wheat

Polygenic inheritance is highly visible in Saskatchewan's agricultural sector. The kernel color of wheat is not simply "red" or "white." It is controlled by up to three separate genes (A, B, and C) working together. The more dominant alleles present across these three genes, the darker red the wheat kernel becomes. Crop scientists at the University of Saskatchewan must understand this complex polygenic interaction to breed specific varieties desired by global markets.

5.3 Predicting Two Traits: The Dihybrid Cross

Learning Objective: Calculate genotypic and phenotypic ratios for two independent traits using a dihybrid cross.

Mendel's early experiments tracked a single trait at a time (a monohybrid cross). But organisms are complex collections of thousands of traits. Mendel's next logical question was: Are different traits inherited together, or independently? Does a pea plant that inherits a dominant seed shape also automatically inherit a dominant flower color?

To test this, he performed a dihybrid cross—an experiment tracking two distinct traits simultaneously. Let's walk through his classic experiment crossing pea shape and flower color.

The P Generation: True-Breeding Parents

Mendel started by crossing a pure-breeding plant with smooth peas and purple flowers (both dominant traits) with a pure-breeding plant with wrinkled peas and white flowers (both recessive traits). Let's define our alleles:

  • Pea Shape: Smooth (S) is dominant to wrinkled (s).
  • Flower Color: Purple (P) is dominant to white (p).

The genotype of the dominant parent is SSPP. The genotype of the recessive parent is sspp.

The F1 Generation: The Double Hybrids

Because the parents are homozygous, the SSPP plant can only pass on 'SP' alleles, and the sspp plant can only pass on 'sp' alleles. When these gametes combine, 100% of the offspring in the F1 generation inherit the exact same genotype: SsPp. Because they possess at least one dominant allele for each trait, their phenotype is 100% Smooth and Purple.

The F2 Generation: The 9:3:3:1 Ratio

The real magic happens when two F1 hybrids (SsPp x SsPp) are crossed. According to Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment, the alleles for seed shape separate independently from the alleles for flower color. Each parent can produce four different gamete combinations (using the FOIL method: First, Outer, Inner, Last): SP, Sp, sP, and sp.

Plotting these four gametes against each other requires a massive 16-square Punnett grid. The resulting F2 offspring showcase a famous mathematical ratio that proved traits do not "stick" together:

  • 9 Smooth & Purple (Genotypes: SSPP, SsPP, SSPp, SsPp)
  • 3 Smooth & White (Genotypes: SSpp, Sspp)
  • 3 Wrinkled & Purple (Genotypes: ssPP, ssPp)
  • 1 Wrinkled & White (Genotype: sspp)

The Ultimate Combination: Human Blood Typing

[Video Feature]: Understanding ABO Blood Types

Explainer

Watch this video to visualize how multiple alleles and codominance interact to determine human blood types before we break down the genetics.

We have explored dihybrid crosses using Mendel's peas, but how does this apply to humans? One of the most critical applications of dihybrid genetics is human blood typing. The inheritance of your blood type is an elegant combination of multiple alleles, codominance, simple dominance, and independent assortment.

1. The ABO Blood Group (Multiple Alleles & Codominance)

Unlike Mendel's pea traits which only had two alleles, the human ABO blood group is controlled by a single gene with three possible alleles in the population: IA, IB, and i.

  • Codominance: Alleles IA and IB are codominant. If you inherit one of each (Genotype: IAIB), your red blood cells will simultaneously express both A and B surface proteins, resulting in Type AB blood.
  • Simple Dominance: Both IA and IB exhibit complete dominance over the recessive i allele. You can have Type A blood with a heterozygous genotype (IAi). To have Type O blood, you must inherit two recessive alleles (ii).
2. The Rh Factor (Simple Dominance)

The positive (+) or negative (-) attached to your blood type comes from an entirely separate gene on a different chromosome that codes for the Rhesus (Rh) protein. This gene follows classic Mendelian simple dominance:

  • Having the Rh protein is dominant (R). Genotypes RR and Rr result in an Rh+ phenotype.
  • Lacking the Rh protein is recessive (r). Only the homozygous recessive genotype rr results in an Rh- phenotype.
Putting it Together: A Dihybrid Cross

Because the ABO gene and the Rh gene are on completely different chromosomes, they assort independently during meiosis. Predicting a child's complete blood type requires a dihybrid cross! For example, if a mother is heterozygous Type A+ (IAi Rr) and a father is heterozygous Type B+ (IBi Rr), their gametes will combine in a massive 16-square Punnett grid. Incredibly, this specific pairing has the genetic potential to produce a child with any of the 8 possible human blood types!

Saskatchewan Connection: The Universal Donor and STARS

In a medical emergency across Saskatchewan's vast rural geography, the STARS air ambulance often administers life-saving blood transfusions mid-flight before ever reaching a hospital in Saskatoon or Regina. They carry exactly one type of blood: Type O-Negative (Genotype: ii rr). Because these red blood cells lack A proteins, B proteins, and Rh proteins, they will not trigger an immune response in any patient, regardless of the patient's own genetic blood type. Only about 7% of Canadians carry this highly sought-after double-recessive genotype, making O- blood donors essential to provincial trauma care.

5.4 Chromosomes, Meiosis, and Mutations [BI30-GB1i, j, k, l]

Learning Objective: Investigate the importance of meiosis and crossing-over in creating variation, and identify chromosomal abnormalities using karyotypes.

Mendel proposed his laws of inheritance (Law of Segregation, Law of Independent Assortment) without ever knowing what a gene or a chromosome was. It wasn't until decades later that scientists discovered that chromosomes inside the nucleus are the actual physical mechanism carrying Mendel's invisible "factors."

Meiosis: The Engine of Diversity

To ensure that offspring have the correct number of chromosomes (46 in humans), organisms must produce sex cells (gametes: sperm and egg) that have exactly half the normal number of chromosomes (23). This reduction division is called Meiosis.

Meiosis doesn't just cut the number in half; it actively scrambles the genetic code to ensure every sperm and egg is entirely unique. During early meiosis, homologous chromosomes pair up and physically swap chunks of DNA in a process called Crossing-Over. This recombination is why you look different from your siblings even though you share the same parents.

Chromosomal Mutations and Nondisjunction

The complex dance of meiosis sometimes experiences errors. If entire sections of a chromosome break or reattach incorrectly, large-scale mutations occur:

  • Deletion: A piece of the chromosome is lost.
  • Duplication: A segment is repeated.
  • Inversion: A segment detaches, flips 180 degrees, and reattaches.
  • Translocation: A piece of one chromosome breaks off and attaches to a completely different, non-homologous chromosome.

An even larger error occurs if the chromosomes fail to separate correctly during anaphase of meiosis. This is called Nondisjunction. It results in a sperm or egg cell having either one too many, or one too few chromosomes. If this gamete is fertilized, the resulting child will have a chromosomal disorder. Scientists diagnose these conditions by taking a picture of the cell's chromosomes, arranging them by size in a chart called a Karyotype.

Disorder Chromosomal Mechanism Karyotype Notation
Down Syndrome Nondisjunction resulting in three copies of Chromosome 21 (Trisomy 21). 47, XX,+21 or 47, XY,+21
Turner Syndrome A female inheriting only one X chromosome (Monosomy X). 45, X0
Klinefelter Syndrome A male inheriting an extra X chromosome. 47, XXY

5.5 Population Genetics [BI30-GB1h]

Learning Objective: Describe how gene flow, genetic drift, and natural selection influence the frequency of alleles within a population.

While Punnett squares predict the traits of individuals, Population Genetics examines the genetics of an entire group over time. Evolution, at a genetic level, is simply a change in the frequency of alleles in a population over generations. Three major mechanisms drive this change:

  1. Natural Selection: The environment applies pressure. Individuals with alleles that provide a survival advantage will live to reproduce, passing those advantageous alleles on, increasing their frequency in the population.
  2. Gene Flow: The movement of alleles into or out of a population due to the migration of individuals. This keeps adjacent populations genetically similar.
  3. Genetic Drift: Random, chance events that change allele frequencies, impacting small populations most severely.
    • The Founder Effect: A small group breaks off from a larger population to start a new colony. The new colony may have a completely different allele frequency than the original population just by random chance.
    • The Bottleneck Effect: A disaster (disease, famine, human hunting) drastically reduces the population size. The few survivors rebuild the population, but massive amounts of genetic diversity have been lost forever.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Bison Bottleneck

Before the 1800s, an estimated 30-60 million Plains Bison roamed North America, including the Saskatchewan prairie. By 1890, due to overhunting and habitat loss, the population was reduced to less than 1,000 individuals. This is a devastating example of the Bottleneck Effect. Even though conservation efforts have brought bison numbers back up into the hundreds of thousands today, modern herds possess only a tiny fraction of the genetic diversity that existed 200 years ago, making them potentially more vulnerable to future diseases or climate shifts.
