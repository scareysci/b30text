A Note on Independent Research

Guide

If you have never done independent research before, take a deep breath. It can feel messy and overwhelming at first. You might read articles that use confusing jargon, or you might fall down a rabbit hole. That is completely normal! This section breaks the research process down into bite-sized, manageable steps.

Step 6.2: Targeted Reading

Read with a mission. Hunt for answers to these questions:

Part A: The "What" (The Science)

  • Micro View: What happens at the cellular/molecular level?
  • Macro View: How does this affect the whole organism or ecosystem?
  • The Scope: What specific part of this massive topic are you focusing on so it doesn't get overwhelming?
  • The Cutting Edge: What is the most recent breakthrough, data, or discovery (within the last 5 years) that makes this interesting?
  • Diverse Perspectives: Are there differing scientific opinions? How do Indigenous knowledge systems or other cultural lenses view this concept?

Part B: The "Why" (The Relevance)

  • Local Impact: How does this connect to Saskatchewan?
  • Future Impact: What are the ethical/environmental impacts 50 years from now?

Step 6.3: The "Read, Hide, Write" Strategy

To prevent accidental plagiarism (copying and pasting), use this brain-friendly strategy:

  1. READ a paragraph or section of your article.
  2. HIDE the article (minimize the tab or turn the page over).
  3. WRITE the main idea in your own words. If you can't explain it without looking, you don't understand it yet!

PHASE 5: Building Your Final Product (Execution)

You have a pile of notes and a completed Source Breakdown. Now what? How do you turn a bunch of facts into a project that people actually want to read, watch, or listen to? Don't just dump data onto a page. Follow these steps to craft an engaging final product.

Step 7.1: Know Your Audience

Before you write a single word, build a model, or record a single minute, answer this: Who is your target audience?

Step 7.2: The "Story" Arc

Every great project—whether it's a podcast, an essay, or a 3D model with a legend—follows a narrative arc. Map out the flow of your project by bringing your Pixar Pitch to life:

1. The Status Quo (Once upon a time & Every day): How will you introduce the baseline science so your audience understands normal, healthy function?

2. The Inciting Incident (One day...): How will you introduce the conflict, mutation, environmental change, or main problem?

3. The Ripple Effect (Because of that...): What are the 2-3 main scientific concepts you *must* explain to show the consequences?
