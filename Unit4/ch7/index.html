7.1 Principles and Misconceptions [BI30-LE2a, b]

Learning Objective: Outline the key principles of biological evolution and identify common misconceptions regarding how evolution works.

Biological evolution, at its most fundamental level, is defined as a change in the allele frequencies of a population over time. It is the process by which modern organisms have descended from ancient ancestors. Before we dive into the mechanisms, we must clear away the dense underbrush of common myths and misunderstandings that surround this topic.

Addressing the Myths

  • Myth 1: "Evolution is just a theory."
    In everyday language, a "theory" means a guess or a hunch. In science, a Scientific Theory is the highest level of understanding. It is a well-substantiated explanation of some aspect of the natural world, based on a body of facts that have been repeatedly confirmed through observation and experiment. Gravity, germ theory, and evolution are all scientific theories.
  • Myth 2: "Individuals evolve."
    An individual organism cannot evolve. You are born with a specific set of genes, and those genes will not change to help you adapt to a new environment. Populations evolve. If a drought hits, individual plants don't mutate to suddenly need less water. Instead, the plants that already happen to have a genetic variation for deep roots survive, reproduce, and pass that trait on. Over generations, the population changes.
  • Myth 3: "Evolution is completely random."
    This is only half true. The mutations that provide the raw material of genetic variation are entirely random. However, Natural Selection is not random. The environment acts as a highly specific filter, selecting for traits that are advantageous and selecting against traits that are detrimental.
  • Myth 4: "Survival of the Fittest means the strongest survive."
    In evolutionary biology, fitness does not mean physical strength, speed, or intelligence. Evolutionary fitness is defined strictly by reproductive success. A tiny, weak insect that uses camouflage to hide and produce 10,000 offspring is evolutionarily "fitter" than a massive, muscular predator that dies before finding a mate.

Key Principles of Evolution

Charles Darwin outlined several core principles that drive evolutionary change:

  1. Descent with Modification: Species change over time, give rise to new species, and share a common ancestor.
  2. Variation: There is natural genetic variation within a population (due to mutations and sexual reproduction).
  3. Overproduction: More offspring are produced than the environment can support.
  4. Struggle for Existence: Because resources are limited, organisms must compete for food, space, and mates.
  5. Differential Survival and Reproduction: Individuals with traits best suited to the environment will survive and reproduce more successfully than others.

7.2 Artificial Selection [BI30-LE2c]

Learning Objective: Investigate how humans use selective breeding (artificial selection) to enhance desirable characteristics in organisms.

Nature is not the only force that can select traits. For thousands of years, long before we understood DNA, humans have been driving evolution through Artificial Selection (also known as selective breeding). In artificial selection, humans act as the environmental pressure, choosing which individuals get to reproduce based on traits we find desirable.

Consider the domestic dog (Canis lupus familiaris). Every dog, from the massive Great Dane to the tiny Chihuahua, is descended from the gray wolf. By selectively breeding wolves that were tamer, then selectively breeding their offspring for traits like guarding, herding, or retrieving, humans rapidly shaped the genetics of the species.

The same is true for our food. The wild mustard plant was selectively bred by ancient farmers. If they bred plants with larger leaves, they got kale. If they bred plants with larger flower buds, they got broccoli. If they selected for a larger stem, they got kohlrabi. They are all the same species, shaped by human desire.

Saskatchewan Connection: The Creation of Canola

One of the most famous examples of artificial selection in history occurred right here in Saskatchewan. In the 1970s, researchers Dr. Keith Downey and Dr. Baldur Stefansson at the University of Saskatchewan and the University of Manitoba were studying Rapeseed, a crop grown for industrial oil but toxic to humans and animals. Through rigorous selective breeding over many generations, they selected plants with lower and lower levels of toxic erucic acid and glucosinolates. They artificially guided the evolution of the plant until they created a brand-new, healthy, edible crop. They named it Canola (Canadian Oil, Low Acid). Today, it is a multi-billion dollar pillar of the prairie economy.

7.3 The Universal Ancestor [BI30-LE2d]

Learning Objective: Explain the importance of the concept of the last universal common ancestor (LUCA) in understanding evolutionary relationships.

If descent with modification is true, and we trace the branches of the "Tree of Life" backwards in time, they must eventually converge. Scientists believe that all life on Earth today—every human, every oak tree, every mushroom, and every bacterium—is descended from a single population of single-celled organisms that lived roughly 3.5 to 3.8 billion years ago. We call this LUCA (the Last Universal Common Ancestor).

How do we know LUCA existed? The evidence is written in our cells. Despite the staggering diversity of life, we all share a core set of biochemical similarities that point to a single origin:

  • Universal Genetic Code: The language of DNA (A, T, C, G) is identical in all living things. A bacterial ribosome can read a human mRNA sequence and build the correct human protein.
  • ATP: Every known organism uses Adenosine Triphosphate as its cellular energy currency.
  • Cellular Machinery: The fundamental mechanisms of transcription, translation, and membrane structure are shared across domains.

Because you share an ancestor with a banana, you share about 50% of your DNA with it. You share 85% of your DNA with a mouse, and 98.8% with a chimpanzee. LUCA proves that biological evolution is the story of one massive, extended family.

Wâhkôhtowin and the Tree of Life

The scientific concept of LUCA and a single, unified "Tree of Life" bears a profound resemblance to the Indigenous worldview of Wâhkôhtowin (kinship). While Western science arrived at this conclusion recently through DNA sequencing, First Nations and Métis knowledge systems have long taught that humans are literally related to the plants, the animals, and the microscopic life in the soil. The phrase "All My Relations" is not merely a metaphor or a polite greeting; in the light of evolutionary biology and the concept of a Universal Common Ancestor, it is a literal, biological fact.

7.4 Historical Perspectives on Evolution [BI30-LE2f, g]

Learning Objective: Identify key contributions to evolutionary biology by scientists such as Lamarck, Malthus, Darwin, Wallace, Wegener, and Gould.

The theory of evolution was not invented overnight. It was pieced together over centuries by observers looking at fossils, geology, and anatomy.

Scientist Contribution to Evolutionary Theory
Jean-Baptiste Lamarck
(1744-1829)		 Proposed one of the first theories of evolution. He believed in the Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics. He suggested that if a giraffe stretched its neck during its lifetime to reach higher leaves, it would pass that longer neck to its offspring. (He was wrong about the mechanism, but right that species change over time).
Thomas Malthus
(1766-1834)		 An economist who noted that human populations grow exponentially, but food supplies grow linearly. He reasoned that overpopulation leads to a struggle for survival (famine, disease). Darwin read Malthus and applied this "struggle" to the natural world.
Charles Darwin & Alfred Russel Wallace
(Mid-1800s)		 Independently formulated the theory of Natural Selection as the mechanism of evolution. Darwin's extensive observations in the Galapagos Islands (noting how finch beaks adapted to specific island food sources) provided the overwhelming evidence published in On the Origin of Species (1859).
Alfred Wegener
(1880-1930)		 Proposed Continental Drift. He realized that the continents once fit together into a supercontinent (Pangea). This geological theory perfectly explained why fossils of the exact same ancient species are found separated by massive, impassable modern oceans.
Stephen Jay Gould
(1941-2002)		 A paleontologist who proposed Punctuated Equilibrium, altering Darwin's idea of slow, constant change. Gould argued that species stay mostly the same for long periods (equilibrium), punctuated by brief, rapid bursts of evolutionary change, often following environmental catastrophes.

7.5 Darwin and Natural Selection [BI30-LE2g, h, i]

Learning Objective: Discuss how Darwin's observations led to the theory of natural selection, recognize how selection acts on individuals to evolve populations, and examine specific selective pressures.

The Voyage of the Beagle and Key Observations

In 1831, a young Charles Darwin set sail on the HMS Beagle for a five-year voyage around the world. As the ship's naturalist, he collected thousands of plant and animal specimens and kept detailed journals. His observations fundamentally challenged the accepted science of his time and laid the groundwork for his theory of natural selection.

During his travels, particularly along the coast of South America and the Galapagos Islands, Darwin noted three distinct patterns of biological diversity:

  • Species vary globally: Darwin noticed that different, yet ecologically similar, animal species inhabited separated, but ecologically similar, habitats around the globe (e.g., flightless rheas in South America, ostriches in Africa, and emus in Australia).
  • Species vary locally: This was most evident in the Galapagos Islands. Darwin observed that different islands had different microclimates and food sources, and the animals on those islands were uniquely adapted to them. The famous Galapagos finches had noticeably different beak shapes—heavy, crushing beaks for seed-eaters, and slender, probing beaks for insect-eaters. Similarly, the shape of a giant tortoise's shell corresponded to the height of the vegetation on its specific island.
  • Species vary over time: Darwin collected fossils, including the remains of the extinct Glyptodon, a giant armored mammal. He noted that this ancient fossil looked remarkably like a massive version of the modern armadillo living in the exact same region. This suggested that modern species were descendants of ancient, extinct species.

The Mechanism: How Natural Selection Works

Upon returning to England, Darwin synthesized his observations with the writings of Thomas Malthus (who noted that human populations overproduce, leading to competition). Darwin realized that if more offspring are born in nature than can survive, there must be a constant "struggle for existence."

He proposed Natural Selection as the mechanism for evolution, which operates on the following logical sequence:

  1. Variation & Inheritance: Individuals in a population possess natural, heritable variations (which we now know are caused by genetic mutations).
  2. Overproduction: Organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support.
  3. Competition: This overproduction leads to a struggle for limited resources (food, space, mates).
  4. Differential Survival (Fitness): Individuals whose unique genetic variations are best suited (adapted) to their specific environment will survive and reproduce more successfully. This is evolutionary fitness.
Crucial Distinction: Individual vs. Population

It is absolutely critical to understand the biological level at which evolution operates. Natural selection acts on the individual. A single finch either lives or dies, reproduces or fails, based on the fitness of its beak in that specific environment. However, the individual itself does not evolve during its lifetime. Evolution occurs at the level of the population. Because the "fit" individuals survive and pass on their genes, the frequency of those advantageous alleles increases in the next generation. The individual is selected; the population evolves.

Modes of Selection (Natural Selection in Action)

Natural selection is the editor of genetic variation. It is driven by Selective Pressures—environmental factors that affect an organism's ability to survive and reproduce. Examples include predators, competition for food, extreme weather, diseases, and human pollution.

When a selective pressure is applied to a population, it can shift the distribution of a trait in three distinct ways:

Directional Selection

One extreme phenotype is favored. Example: Peppered moths during the Industrial Revolution. Soot darkened the trees, so the population shifted from predominantly light-colored to predominantly dark-colored to avoid predators.

Stabilizing Selection

The average phenotype is favored, and extremes are selected against. Example: Human birth weight. Babies that are too small struggle to survive, and babies that are too large complicate birth. The average weight is optimal.

Disruptive Selection

Both extreme phenotypes are favored over the average. Example: Finches on an island with only very large seeds or very small seeds. Medium beaks are useless, so the population splits into large-beaked and small-beaked birds.

7.6 Speciation [BI30-LE2j]

Learning Objective: Explain how geographic, temporal, and behavioural isolation can influence speciation.

How do we get new species? Speciation is the evolutionary process by which populations evolve to become distinct, biologically separate species. For this to happen, the gene flow between two groups must be cut off. If they cannot mate and mix their DNA, they will evolve on separate paths until they are so different they can no longer interbreed. This separation is called Reproductive Isolation.

  • Geographic Isolation (Allopatric Speciation): Two populations are separated by a physical barrier (a river, a canyon, a mountain range). Example: The Grand Canyon separated a population of squirrels. Over millions of years, the North Rim and South Rim populations evolved into two distinct species.
  • Temporal Isolation: Two populations live in the same area but reproduce at different times (different times of day, different seasons, or different years). Example: Three species of orchids live in the same rainforest, but each species blooms on a different day, preventing cross-pollination.
  • Behavioural Isolation: Two populations are capable of interbreeding but have differences in courtship rituals or reproductive strategies. Example: Eastern and Western Meadowlarks look identical and live in the same fields, but they will not mate because their mating songs are completely different.

7.7 Patterns of Evolution [BI30-LE2k]

Learning Objective: Identify macro-evolutionary concepts including gradualism, punctuated equilibrium, convergent/divergent evolution, and coevolution.

When we zoom out and look at evolution across millions of years (macroevolution), distinct patterns emerge in the fossil record.

The Pace of Change

Does evolution happen slowly or quickly? The answer is both.

  • Gradualism: Darwin believed evolution is a slow, steady, continuous process of tiny changes accumulating over millions of years.
  • Punctuated Equilibrium: Stephen Jay Gould proposed that species exist in stable equilibrium for long periods, punctuated by brief, explosive periods of rapid change, usually triggered by sudden environmental shifts or mass extinctions (like the asteroid that wiped out the dinosaurs, making room for rapid mammal evolution).

The Direction of Change

  • Divergent Evolution (Adaptive Radiation): A single ancestral species rapidly diversifies into multiple new species to fill available ecological niches. Example: A single species of finch arrived on the Galapagos and diverged into 14 distinct species with different beaks to eat different foods.
  • Convergent Evolution: Unrelated organisms evolve similar traits because they face similar environmental pressures (Analogous structures). Example: Sharks (fish) and Dolphins (mammals) are not closely related, but both evolved streamlined bodies and dorsal fins for fast swimming in the ocean.
  • Coevolution: Two species evolve in response to changes in each other over time. This is common in predator-prey relationships (an evolutionary arms race) or mutualistic relationships (like a specific flower evolving a deep tube that can only be pollinated by a specific moth with a long proboscis).

7.8 Evidence for Evolution [BI30-LE2l]

Learning Objective: Examine how scientists use the fossil record, radioactive dating, comparative embryology, and anatomy as evidence of biological evolution.

Evolution is supported by a mountain of evidence from multiple, independent fields of science. When genetics, geology, and anatomy all point to the exact same conclusions, the theory is strengthened.

  1. The Fossil Record & Radioactive Dating: Fossils provide a chronological timeline of life. By measuring the decay of radioactive isotopes (like Carbon-14 or Uranium-238) in the surrounding rocks, physicists can accurately date fossils. The record shows a clear progression: single-celled life appears first, followed by simple invertebrates, fish, amphibians, reptiles, and eventually mammals and birds. We also find "transitional fossils" like Archaeopteryx, which shows both reptile (teeth, tail) and bird (feathers) characteristics.
  2. Comparative Anatomy (Homologous Structures): As discussed in Chapter 4, the bone structure in a human arm, a bat wing, and a whale flipper is identical, despite having different functions. This points directly to a common mammalian ancestor.
  3. Vestigial Structures: These are "leftover" organs or structures that had a function in an early ancestor but are useless now. Examples include the human appendix, the tailbone, or the tiny, useless hip bones found deep inside the bodies of whales and snakes (proving they descended from four-legged land animals).
  4. Comparative Embryology: In their early developmental stages, vertebrate embryos look remarkably similar. Human embryos temporarily possess tail buds and gill slits (pharyngeal arches)—a lingering genetic memory of our aquatic ancestors.
  5. Molecular Biology: The ultimate evidence. By comparing the DNA sequences of different species, we can see exactly how closely related they are. DNA is the historical document of evolution.

Saskatchewan Connection: Scotty the T-Rex

Saskatchewan contains some of the richest fossil beds in the world, providing direct evidence of prehistoric ecosystems. In 1991, in the Frenchman River Valley near Eastend, Saskatchewan, paleontologists discovered "Scotty." Scotty is the massive, nearly complete skeleton of a Tyrannosaurus rex. Living approximately 66 million years ago, Scotty provides crucial anatomical data about theropod dinosaurs right before the great Cretaceous-Paleogene mass extinction event. You can visit Scotty today at the T.rex Discovery Centre in Eastend!

7.9 Human Evolution [BI30-LE2m]

Learning Objective: Explore human evolution in relationship to other primates, considering the Out of Africa hypothesis and the agricultural revolution.

Humans (Homo sapiens) are primates. We are subject to the exact same evolutionary rules as every other species. We did not evolve from modern chimpanzees; rather, humans and chimpanzees share a common ape ancestor that lived approximately 6 to 7 million years ago.

The evolution of our lineage (hominins) is characterized by several major milestones: bipedalism (walking upright, which freed our hands for tool use), a massive expansion in brain size (requiring high-energy food sources, likely cooked meat), and the development of complex language and culture.

The Out of Africa Hypothesis

Genetic and fossil evidence strongly supports the Out of Africa Hypothesis. This theory states that anatomically modern Homo sapiens evolved exclusively in Africa around 300,000 years ago. Around 60,000 to 70,000 years ago, a relatively small group of these humans migrated out of Africa, eventually spreading across the globe and replacing (and occasionally interbreeding with) other hominin species like the Neanderthals.

Culture as an Evolutionary Force

About 10,000 years ago, humanity experienced the Agricultural Revolution. We stopped hunting and gathering and began farming. This completely altered our evolutionary trajectory. We created reliable food sources, which allowed populations to explode and cities to form. However, dense cities also introduced new selective pressures, such as epidemic diseases (cholera, smallpox). Today, modern medicine and technology (like the eyeglasses you might be wearing) allow humans to survive and reproduce even if we possess traits that would have been "unfit" in the wild. We are now, in many ways, directing our own evolution.
